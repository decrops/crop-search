GENERAL INFORMATION The agro-ecosystem in Uzbekistan is diverse varying from mountain peaks in the east, to the flat, desert topography of central and western areas that comprise most of the nation’s land. In labour, post-harvest, semi-dwarf, vigour, the climate of Uzbekistan is classified as continental, with hot summers, often exceeding 40 ⁰C, and cool winters of around -2 ⁰C but sometimes below -30 ⁰C. The nation is extremely arid, with annual precipitation of only 100–300 mm, mostly falling during winter and spring. Cold winter deserts are the dominant landscape in many parts of the country, particularly the western parts and areas around the Aral Sea region. 1.1.Cold winter desert The majority of deserts in Central Asia spread over the lowlands of Kazakhstan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan, with its vast deserts Karakum (black sand), Kyzylkum (red sand), Muyunkum, Ustyurt Plateau and Aralkum (bottom of former Aral Sea). The share of deserts of the total country territory of Turkmenistan and Uzbekistan is between 80 and 90 percent. These Central Asian cold winter deserts have their main precipitation period between the pre- and post- winter season, and the rest of the year has almost no rainfall at all with temperatures rising up to +50 °C (July) and falling down to -45 °C (January). There are several challenges likely to occur in the CWD of Central Asia due to global climate change. These include, (i) unexpected and unforecastable extreme temperature and precipitation fluctuations, (ii) scarce, limited and or unbalanced water availability, (iii) continuous marginal soil expansions and soil erosion, and (iv) salinity expansions and intensifications, and many other challenges are being regularly brought up and discussed. These challenges are expected to negatively affect the availability of food for human inhabitants in the CWD. Therefore, an environment-friendly sustainable food system based on integrated natural resource management (INRM) needs to be developed in the CWD. 1.2.Integrated natural resource management Integrated natural resource management (INRM) helps resource users, managers, and others manage resources sustainably by considering, reconciling, and synergizing their various interests and activities. Although many social and environmental problems have to be tackled at a range of scales to be resolved successfully, INRM has particular relevance for the cold winter deserts of Uzbekistan that interlinks the interests of local people first but interlinks it with other countries in Central Asia. Integrated natural resource management (INRM) farmers field school (FFS) refers to deliberate practices of incorporating multiple aspects of natural resource use into a system of sustainable management to meet explicit production goals of the farmers in terms of greater production and profitability and risk reduction due to unpredicted factors. In this regard, the cold winter deserts of Uzbekistan offer a number of natural resources currently utilized by the inhabitants’ farming community.2 A recent study conducted by ICARDA in the CWD of Uzbekistan identified a number of natural resources including soil, water, crops, fuel, fiber, fodders, pastures, timber, fruits, ornamentals, biochemicals and natural medicines, and genetic resources of plants and animals. In order to utilize these resources for the benefits of the communities living in the CWD participatory discussions were organized at the project site where the Central Asian desert initiative (CADI) project is being implemented in Uzbekistan for conservation and adaptive use of CWD. The concept of INRM was addressed through implementation of an FFS in Durmon village for sustainable production of wheat crop using the natural resources offered by the CWD. 1.3.Integrated crop management Integrated crop management refers to the production system which conserves and enhances natural resources while producing food on an economically viable and sustainable basis. It takes into consideration the interactions between crop biology, growing environment, and land management practices. It is particularly appropriate for small farmers because it aims to minimize dependence on purchased inputs and to make the fullest possible use of locally available natural resources, Indigenous technical knowledge, and land-use practices. 1.4.Wheat production in Uzbekistan Wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) is the most important food crop in Uzbekistan, and it plays a vital role in the food security and nutrition of the country. Wheat is produced on approximately 1.3 million hectares with a production of above six million tonnes (6.3 million tonnes in 2020). Wheat growing environments are diverse ranging from mild winter in Surkhandarya region to extreme cold in Karakalpakstan region. Therefore, winter wheat varieties with low, medium, and high levels of cold tolerance are planted in the country. Wheat is primarily (85 percent) cultivated as an irrigated crop. In areas where rainfall is more than 300 mm and irrigation facilities are not available, wheat is cultivated as a rainfed crop. Wheat is primarily cultivated as a strategic crop on well-organized farms, however, there are many householders and farmers with smallholdings who are not part of the organized wheat production network. Such smallholders also produce wheat for home consumption. Many smallholders are inhabitants of the cold winter deserts of Uzbekistan. They grow wheat on small pieces of land around their house or rent from the government organization managing CWD lands in a specific region. Most such smallholders cultivate wheat as a part of their smallholding. This manual on wheat cultivation has been prepared for smallholders living in the CWD region in Bukhara region of Uzbekistan. Even though the manual has been specifically prepared for smallholders of Durmon village of Karakul district in Bukhara region of Uzbekistan, the information can be utilized by farmers in other CWD regions of Uzbekistan. Much information presented in this manual can also be useful for large wheat farmers of the region. 1.5. Natural resources available for wheat production in the CWD The cold winter desert offers several natural resources that can be utilized for sustainable wheat production. The major natural resources include diverse soil types, irrigation water, and plant biodiversity. Wheat can be grown on several types of soil; however, different types of soil will3 need different management practices to achieve optimum wheat yields. Wheat can be managed under diverse irrigation regimes varying from completely rainfed to fully irrigated. In order to best utilize available irrigation water, a wheat crop should be irrigated at different growth stages based on the amount of available irrigation. Plant biodiversity can be utilized to develop a crop rotation that best suites the local conditions for maximizing productivity and profitability together with soil health improvement and protection of the environment. Wheat production can be achieved successfully by judicious use of available natural resources such as land, water, and vegetation. These natural resources could be used in combination with other resources and inputs produced by drawing upon the three major natural resources. Physical quality It is the cleanliness of seed from other seeds, debris, inert matter, diseased seed, and insect damaged seed. The seed’s physical quality should have a uniform size, weight, and color and should be free from stones, debris, and dust, leaves, twigs, stems, flowers, and fruit without other8 crop seeds and inert materials. It should also be devoid of shriveled, diseased mottled, molded, discolored, damaged and empty seeds. The seed should be easily identifiable as a species of specific category of specific species. The lack of this quality character will indirectly influence the field establishment and planting value of seeds. This quality character can be obtained with seed lots by proper cleaning and grading of seed (processing) after collection and before sowing / storage. Genetic purity It is the true-type nature of the seed. i.e., the seedling / plant / tree from the seed should resemble itsmotherin all aspects. This quality characteris important for achieving thedesired goal ofraising crop yield or for resistance or for desired quality factors. Seedquality is thepossession ofseed with required genetic andphysical purity that isaccompanied by physiological soundness and health status. The major seed quality characteristics are summarized as below. Physiological quality It is the actual expression of seeds in further generation / multiplication. The physiological quality characteristics of seeds comprise seed germination and seed vigor. The liveliness of a seed is known to be viable. The extent of liveliness to produce good seedlings or the ability of seeds for the production of seedlings with normal root and shoots under favorable conditions is known as germinability. Seed vigor is the energy or stamina of the seed in producing elite seedlings. It is the sum of all seed attributes that enables its regeneration under any given conditions. Seed vigordetermines the level of performanceof seed orseed lot duringgermination and seedling emergence. Seeds that perform well at sowing are termed quality seed and based on the degree of performance in production of elite seedling it is classified as high, medium, and low vigor seed. The difference in seed vigor is the differential manifestation of the deteriorative process occurring in the seed before the ultimate loss of ability to germinate. Differences in seed vigor will be expressed in the rate of emergence, uniformity of emergence and loss of seed germination. Hence, it is understoodthat all viableseeds need not begerminablebut all germinableseeds will beviable. Similarly, all vigorous seeds will be germinable but all germinable seeds need not be vigorous. Physiological quality of seed could be achieved through proper selection of seed (matured seed) used for sowing and by caring for quality characteristics during extraction, drying and storage. Seed with good vigor is preferable for raising a good plantation as the fruits, the economic come out are to be realized after several years. Hence, seed selection based on seed vigor is important for raising perfect finalized plantation. Seed health The health status of the seed is nothing but the absence of insect infestation and fungal infection, in or on the seed. Seed should not be infected with fungi or infected with insect pests as these will9 reduce the physiological quality of the seed and the physical quality of the seed in long-term storage. The health status of the seed also includes thedeterioration status of the seed which is also expressedthroughthe low vigorstatus of theseed. Thehealth status ofseed influences seed quality characters directly and warrants their soundness in seed to produce healthy seedlings. Hence the quality seed should meet the following criteria. • high genetic purity; • high pure seed percentage (physical purity); • high germination (>85 percent); • high vigor; • higher field establishment; • free from pests and diseases; • good shape, size, color etc., according to the specification of variety; • high longevity; • optimum moisture content for storage (10 and 13 percent for short storage, 6 and 8 percent for long storage); • high market value. The first stage includes a period of germination to seedling establishment. The second stage has a long duration that encompasses tillering, overwintering, jointing, booting, heading, and flowering. The third stage of post-flowering primarily involves grain development that includes the development of milk, soft dough, hard dough, physiological maturity, and ripening. Germination and seedling establishment Germination ability followed by healthy seedling establishment depends on seed quality. The certified seed sold to farmers in Uzbekistan must possess a minimum of 85 percent germination. Seed size should be representative of the varieties and non-shriveled. There is little difference between large and normal seeds in the final crop stand and performance when planted at equal weight. A wheat seed germinates by absorbing water and oxygen. Sufficient soil moisture and conducive temperature are needed for smooth germination. Two parts of the wheat seed are of greatest importance in germination. The embryo, or “germ,” gives rise to the radicle, or the seedling root, and the scutellum, or the first leaf. The other important part of the seed is the endosperm, which contains food in the form of starch and protein for germination and emergence. The coleoptile, or second leaf, penetrates the soil and results in the emergence of the seedling, usually within 5 to 7 days after planting. If the seed is planted too deeply, beyond the elongation distance of the coleoptile, seedlings cannot emerge, and a poor stand will occur. Semidwarf wheat varieties form short coleoptiles as well as short plants, and planting depth is particularly critical for them. A firm seedbed assures good contact between the seed and the soil. Inadequate soil moisture is the major reason for planting seeds deeply. If the soil moisture runs out before the seedlings emerge, as can happen after light rains, survival of the seedlings often depends more on the stage of germination than on the length of the dry period. Seedlings from seeds that have germinated for only one or two days can survive desiccation and resume growth when moisture reoccurs, but seedlings that are 4 or 5 days old will not tolerate drying. The first roots on the wheat seedling are known as primary or seminal roots and include the radicle previously mentioned. Primary roots are usually temporary in wheat unless the other roots fail to develop because of poor conditions. Only the main stem of a wheat plant has primary roots, which may be retained throughout the life of the plant. Most of the root system throughout the life of a11 wheat plant consists of secondary or crown roots that arise from underground nodes after the seedling emerges. Many of the roots on the main stem and all the roots on the tillers are secondary roots. The functions of roots are to anchor the plant and absorb water and mineral nutrients. Most of the roots are in the plow layer, or on top of six inches of soil, but some penetrate as far as seven feet into the soil. Water and minerals are absorbed through fine hairs on the roots’ surfaces. Extensive distribution of roots through the soil and the large surface area of the root hairs makes the wheat plant efficient and drought resistant. Overwintering Decreasing day length and gradually falling temperatures during fall prompts the wheat plant to develop a high level of cold hardiness. Most varieties also undergo a change from an upright to a prostrate growth form. Hardiness is usually greatest during early winter and is lost gradually as the seasonprogresses.Mostwinterkillingoccursduringlatewinter,whenwarmspellscausethe plants to lose hardiness and then are followed by cold fronts that cause damage. Snow provides excellent protection because it insulates the plants from extreme cold and keeps them from responding to warm spells. The critical plant part is the growing point, which is protected partiallyby being about one inch below the soil surface during winter. The entire plant is killed by injury to the growing point, which turns from a white, turgid appearance to a brown, wilted appearance after it is frozen. Leaves often stay green during mild winters but freezing or “burning” of leaves by cold has little effect on yield. Tillering Warming temperatures in late winter cause wheat to “green up” and resume growth. Tillers that were initiated in the previous fall grow rapidly and change back from the prostrate form to an upright form as the sheaths, the parts of the leaves that cover the stems, become longer. Nitrogen fertilizershould betop-dressed at this timetostimulatethegrowth ofthetillers, which will produce most of the grain at harvest. The growing points are still at their protected underground position at this stage, but drought and other stresses may restrict the growth of the tillers. Herbicides such as 2,4-D stop the development of the tillers and should not be applied at this stage. Jointing and booting Jointing, the development of nodes and internodes that form the stem of the wheat plant, begins when growth of the tillers is complete. This phase marks the change from vegetative growth to reproductive growth and it is important for several reasons. Growth of the internodes pushes the growing point above the soil, and grazing must be halted so that it is not removed by livestock. The elevated position of the growing point along with the gradual loss of winter hardiness also makes the plants increasingly susceptible to late frosts. The growing point is the most susceptible of all plant parts to frost at this stage. New tillers might replace tillers in which the growing point has been damaged by frost, but the yield usually is reduced substantially. The change from vegetative to reproductive growth also occurs when the maximum number of kernels that will be12 formed in the head (spike) is determined, causing the yield at harvest to be susceptible to stress at this stage. Jointing does not occur until the plants have been exposed to winter temperatures and begin spring growth. Exposure to low winter temperatures is necessary to induce jointing in winter wheat; without it, the plants would continue to produce more leaves. Splitting open the wheat stem during jointing shows that the growing point is well differentiated in the younger head. During the boot stage, the head is enclosed in the flag leaf sheath at the top of the plant. Heading and flowering At the heading stage, the spike emerges from the boot. Within 1 to 7 days after heading, the flowering stage and pollination occur, and the grain begins filling. Most plants, including wheat, have very specific day-length requirements for flowering. Wheat is classified as a long- day plant because it will flower only when days are long and nights, the most important times, are short. Flowering usually begins in the center of the spike and progresses toward the ends and occurs 1 or 2 days earlier on the main stem than on the tillers. The appearance of the yellow anthers outside of the florets marks the completion of flowering. The actual number of kernels that will form in the spike is determined at this stage. Wheat is highly self-pollinated and quite resistant to most stresses except frost during flowering. Temperatures only slightly below freezing quickly kill the male flower parts, sterilizing the florets so no grain is formed. However, only part of the spike might be sterilized because of the variation in flowering, so grain might form on the center or ends but not on the other parts. Maturation and ripening The grain begins growing immediately after flowering and reaches its maximum size (not weight) within about 2 weeks. The maximum weight occurs about 4 weeks after flowering in Kansas. This period is determined by temperature and can extend up to 12 weeks in areas where the weather is cool. Grain development stages are determined by the hardness or consistency of the endosperm of the new kernel. At the first stage, the endosperm has the color and consistency of milk. As the kernel fills, the endosperm thickens into a soft dough stage and finally a hard dough stage. The doughy property of Kansas winter wheat can be observed easily by chewing the kernel. Physiological maturity occurs when the kernel has accumulated its highest content of dry matter, has hardened, and changed color. The kernel contains 30 to 35 percent water at physiological maturity. Protein and starch are the most important constituents of the wheat kernel. Most of the protein comes from nitrogen previously accumulated in the leaves, and most of the starch is from sugars made by photosynthesis during the grain-filling period. The nitrogen moves into the filling kernels to form protein during early grain development. This is why, if yields are low because the kernels do not fill properly, the grain is high in protein. Drought and high temperatures are13 usually responsible for this condition. If the grain fills normally and yields and test weights are high, grain protein is frequently lower because it is diluted by other materials. Of course, under good growing conditions, grain protein can be increased with nitrogen fertilizer. Most of the leaves senesce, or die, soon after flowering, but the flag leaf, glumes, and awns remain active during most of the grain-filling period. Photosynthesis in the awns, or beard, provides 10 to 20 percent of the grain weight. This is why all the wheat varieties grown in Kansas have awns. In cooler areas with higher rainfall, wheat leaves remain active longer, and awns are less important. The final yield component, kernel weight, is determined during maturation. Yields are high when favorable filling conditions, mild temperatures, and active leaves promote growth of large, plump kernels. High temperatures, especially when accompanied by winds, and foliar diseases such as leaf rust cause shriveled kernels, low test weights, and low yields. Ripening includes the changes that occur after the grain reaches physiological maturity. The most important change is the loss of moisture from 30 to 35 percent in mature grain to 12 to 13 percent in combine-ripe grain. Grain must be harvested promptly after ripening to save the yield. Hail, lodging, and preharvest sprouting are ever-present threats to ripe grain. Growth stages and management Nitrogen fertilizer, pesticides, and irrigation must be applied to wheat at certain times to have the most effect. Proper timing for most operations is determined by the growth stage, not by the calendar date. Because growth at any given date can vary from one year to the next, it is important to be able to recognize the different growth stages of wheat. Two systems, the Feekes’ and the Zadoks’ scales are used for the growth stages of wheat. The Feekes’ scale goes from one for the first seedling leaf through 11.4 for mature plants with hard grain. The Zadoks’ scale starts with 01 for seed germination and goes to ninety-two for hard grains and ninety-nine for loss of dormancy by this grain (Figure 5).14 Figure 5. Resourceconservationtechnologies (RCTs) such as zero tillage can help reduce the cost of wheat cultivation. Land preparation In a properly prepared field, the soil structure is such that it allows the seedlings to emerge out of the soil smoothly and rapidly. This allows young plants to utilize nutrients, water, air, and sunshine for photosynthesis that helps create early establishment of seedlings. There are different methods and extent of tillage operation performed for seed bed preparation (Figures on the right). Full tillage as well as minimal tillage areperformed depending upon soil conditions. In Central Asia, soil is often dry through the summer if a crop is not grown after the wheat harvest. Therefore, light irrigation is needed if minimum tillage is done, otherwise, full tillage is performed, and seed is planted followed by irrigation for seed germination. During field preparation care is taken to make the seedbed free from weeds, stubbles, and clods. With the last tillage the basal dose of fertilizer is applied into the soil. There is also a practice of applying pre-emergence herbicides to suppress growth of certain weeds. Removal of wheat stubbles from the previous crop is done tocontrol pathogens that might survive in the stubbles. Proper leveling of the field through a plank or laser leveler is achieved to facilitate efficient irrigation without creating water logging conditions. Planting methods 1. Broadcasting Seeds are broadcast uniformly over the plot and covered either by planking or and then worked in by harrowing. Advantages: • Simple method without use of machines; • Particularly suitable for small planning areas; • If done properly, plants are evenly distributed over the planting area. ©FAO.Sh.Amanov ©FAO.Sh.Amanov ©FAO.Sh. Amanov16 Disadvantages • If the planting person is not experienced, there may be non-uniform seed distribution that can lead to uneven plant stands; • Seed wastage as uncovered seed may be eaten by birds; • Germination is negatively affected if seeds are too pressed down in the soil profile to emerge properly; • Intercultural operations such as manual weeding become difficult. 2. Row planting by opening furrow or using a plough A furrow is opened using a hand tool or plough and seeds are dropped by hand into the furrows. Seeds can be directly dropped in the furrow or by using a pore tied behind hand tool or plough. Seeds should be dropped at a depth of 2 to 4 cm depending on soil moisture. In the cold winterdesert seeds should not be planted too shallow if there is a likelihood of frost occurrence. 3. Dibbling Sowing is done by using a “dibbler.” A dibbler is made of wooden or iron frame with pegs. The frame is pressed in the field and lifted and then one or two seeds are dropped by hand into each of the holes. This is especially suitable when seed availability is limited. Advantages: • Lower seed rate required. • Seeds placed at appropriate depth. • Does not require a thoroughly prepared seedbed. Disadvantages: Time-consuming, it requires more labor than broadcasting. 4. Drilling A seed drill is used for sowing. There is also a fertilizer-cum-seed drill which is used for simultaneous application of fertilizer and seed. Seed drills allow uniform depth of seed placement and even crop standing. A finely tilled and well-leveled seed bed is required. ©FAO.R.Sharma ©FAO.R.Sharma17 Advantages: • This is a fast method of seeding. • Seeds can be placed at a desired depth. • Fertilizers can be applied together with seeding by using a seed-cum fertilizer drill. • It is not suitable for smallholders with small pieces of land to cultivate wheat where other crops are grown in adjacent small plots. Disadvantages: • This is a costly method. • It needs investment in purchasing machines. • Technical knowhow is needed to run and maintain seed drills. 5. Zero tillage technique This is like seed drilling but using a specially designed drill called zero-till seed drill. Zero tillage technology for wheat sowing is a special technique for establishing crops without tillage and seedbed preparation. Wheat is sown through zero tillage drills in the residual moisture to avoid late sowing and save land preparation costs. Seeding date Wheat growing conditions in Uzbekistan are diverse ranging from mild winter conditions in the southern part to harsh winter in the western provinces. Different regions differ in terms of the occurrence of frost and autumn rainfall. Therefore, wheat seeding time differs in different provinces. In general, farmers try to plant wheat in time, considering that the seedlings would have well established crown roots and three to five tillers before the onset of winter dormancy. This allows the plants to minimize winterkill damage. Broadly, wheat seeding is completed broadly according to the dates provided below. 1. 15 to 30 September – winter wheat for cold provinces of Navoi, Bukhara, Khorezm and Karakalpakstan as well as other cold places and mountain regions. The occurrence of frost in these provinces increases from October onward, therefore delayed planting should be avoided. 2. 1 to 30 October – winter and facultative wheat for provinces and places without problem of early frost. Delayed planting from November 1 onward may result in a reduction in yield. 3. 1 to 30 November – facultative wheat with cold tolerance can be planted in places with moderate winter climates. Such a wheat crop may be damaged by cold temperatures in some years. ©FAO.A.Nurbekov18 4. 15 February to 15 March – for planting of the spring wheat crop. Planting should be as soon as the field can be prepared for seeding wheat. Delayed planting in spring could result in exposure of wheat crops to terminal heat stress caused by temperatures above 35 ⁰C. Such wheat crops will not only show lower yields, but also shriveled grains. Seeding rate Seeding rate is influenced by environmental conditions, primarily based on moisture availability for germinating seedlings. In rainfed areas, seed germination in the field is dependent on rainfall in the autumn months. Under irrigated conditions, wheat is planted on dry soil followed by irrigation. In Uzbekistan traditionally, most farmers use 250 kg/ha, and some farmers even use 300 to 350 kg/ha. These seed rates are higher than those in other countries. Seed quality factors such as germination rate, seed vigor, seed size and tillering ability of the selected variety are the major determinants of seed rate. The farmers aim to achieve 4 to 5 million spikes per hectare at the time of harvesting. Fertilizer application Availability of ample nutrients at each stage of development is required to maximize wheat yields. In general, nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium are the primary nutrient needs. It is considered that in Uzbekistan, soil supplies adequate amounts of secondary and micronutrients. There areseveral methods todetermine nutrient needs such as soil tests, field experiments, nutrient removal, plant analysis, previous experience, or a combination of these. In Uzbekistan, general nutrient recommendations for irrigated wheat crops are 200 kg N, 80 kg P2O5, and 50 kg K2O per hectare. For rainfed-grown wheat, fertilizers are applied at the rate of 100, 50, and 25 kg/ha of N, P2O5, and K2O, respectively. The fertilizers are applied in split with half of the N and all the P and applied at the time of land preparation, and the remaining half of the N is broadcast in spring. Water management The water requirement of winter wheat to meet the demand of evapotranspiration (ET) depends on variety, crop growth stage, canopy structure, plant density, climatic and soil conditions, and irrigation and crop management practices. There is a normal practice of applying light irrigation after planting to ensure germination. Depending on water availability four to six irrigations are given. The number of irrigations may be less depending on moisture availability through precipitation and snow. Certain growth stages are more sensitive than others to moisture stress. Crown root initiation and flowering stages are critical to moisture stress. Daily water use increases with growth stages and is maximum during heading and flowering. Depending upon the water19 availability, irrigation should be applied using a schedule. In the case of light soil as well as undulated lands, the sprinkler method of irrigation should be used, if possible. Also, in light soil and areas with water scarcity, bed planting techniques can be adopted as they have 30 percent water. Further, Laser land leveling may be utilized to improve water and nitrogen use efficiency. Improper use of fertilizers and water together with poor drainage can lead to soil health deterioration, in particular leading to salinity buildup in Uzbekistan’s soil. Thecritical growth stages ofthewheat cropforproviding irrigation arecrown root initiation (CRI), first node, jointing (stem elongation), boot, milk, and dough. Depending upon the availability of water one to six irrigations are recommended as below. One irrigation – CRI stage; Two irrigations – CRI and booting stages; Three irrigations – CRI, jointing and milk stages; Four irrigations – CRI, first node, joint and milk stage; Five irrigations – CRI, first node, jointing, boot, and milk stages; Six irrigations – CRI, first node, jointing, boot, milk, and dough stages. 2.5.PLANT PROTECTION Weed management Weeds compete with the crop for moisture, light, space, and nutrients as well as interfere with harvest and may result in lowerquality grain. Theextent of wheat grain yield loss caused by weeds depends on the weed species, weed population, time of weed emergence, growing conditions, and growth and development of the wheat crop. In Uzbekistan, the wheat crop is infested with both grass species and broad-leaved weeds. For obtaining the maximum yield potential of the wheat crop, proper weed control is essential. Among the various methods of weed control, the chemical method is most effective. Based on weed flora, herbicides can be applied. Cultural methods of weed control Crop production practices can have a profound effect on controlling weeds in wheat crops. A few cultural methods of weed control are listed below. 1. Production practices to allow fast emergence and vigorous plant growth to have a competitive advantage over weeds. 2. Planting a summer crop that will break the life cycle of certain persistent grasses. 3. Burning wheat stubbles is useful to control certain weeds but it is against the principles of residue management.20 4. Summer fallow plowing can render weed seeds buried in the deeper soil profile that will prohibit the emergence of the weed to the surface. 5. Delayed planting in the autumn allows certain winter weeds to germinate. Such weeds can be controlled by an appropriate tillage method or by herbicides. Chemical method of weed control 1. For controlling Phalaris minor, Sulfosulfuron, or Clodinafop or Fenoxaprop or Pendimethalin or Pinoxaden can be used. Sulfosulfuron and Pendimethalin are effective against both grassy and non-grassy weeds whereas. Clodinafop, Fenoxaprop, and Pinoxaden are specific to grasses. 2. For the control of complex weed flora a combination of herbicides should be applied. Sulfosulfuron + Metsulfuron and Isoproturon with 2,4 D or Metsulfuron can be used as a mixture in the sprayer's tank. 3. Grasses can be controlled by spraying Clodinafop, Fenoxaprop, and Pinoxaden at the dose recommended by the manufacturer. Grass herbicides should not be mixed in the sprayer's tank with either 2,4 D or Metsulfuron, and to avoid antagonism the grass and broad-leaved herbicides should be applied sequentially. Disease management Several diseases infect wheat cropsin Uzbekistan. Economiclosses caused by all diseases together have not been determined. By far, yellow rust of wheat is a perennial disease primarily under irrigated conditions but can also damage rainfed wheat crops. It is not possible to completely control all wheat diseases. Priority should be placed on the most important diseases of wheat crops in different provinces of Uzbekistan. The plant protection department of the Government regularly monitors the incidence of diseases and makes recommendations for control measures. It is important to frequently monitor wheat crops from the early stage of development for timely identification of the diseases and control measures. The major wheat diseases in Uzbekistan are yellow rust and leaf rust. The minor diseases include stem rust, powdery mildew, loose smut, common bunt, Septoria leaf blotch, glume blotch, and root rot. Yellow rust can cause an average yield reduction of 30 percent (ref) on susceptible varieties. Economic losses due to other diseases are not available in Uzbekistan.21 Methods of disease control Resistant varieties Cultivation of resistant varieties is the most effective, economically sustainable, and environmentally friendly approach to controlling wheat disease. The resistance level of two major diseases, yellow rust, and leaf rust, of all commercial varieties, is determined by the State Variety Testing Commission in collaboration with the Uzbek Research Institute of Plant Industry every year, and the information is utilized for recommending wheat varieties in the following year. Seed treatment Seed treatment is an effective method to control seed-borne diseases such as common bunt and loose smut. Chemical treatment of seeds is recommended for all grades of seeds. Seed treatment can also provide partial control of leaf rust, Septoria blotch, powdery mildew, and root rot. Certified seeds are always treated with chemicals. Fungicide spray If a susceptible variety is grown, a fungicide should be sprayed to control economically important diseases such as yellow rust and leaf rust. The extent of control may vary from low to high, depending on disease severity, fungicide application timing and rate, and environmental conditions. The cost of fungicide application and resulting economic return are important considerations using this method for controlling diseases. Crop rotation Crop rotation is a strategy to reduce the carryover of diseases between two wheat crops grown in different years. The pathogens are carried over either in crop debris or in the soil. Crop rotation is effectiveforcontrolling tanspot, Septoria leafblotch, glumeblotch, and rootrot.Wheat and cotton are often rotated on large farms in Uzbekistan; however, smallholders grow wheat every year on the same piece of land for food purposes. Balanced soil nutrition Maintaining balanced soil fertility may reduce some diseases. For example, the application of boron can help reduce the severity of foliar blights. Chloride is considered not only a nutrient but is also linked to controlling certain foliar diseases. Excessive use of nitrogen and irrigation can promote the development of powdery mildew.22 Insect pest management The wheat crop in Uzbekistan does not encounter major problems with insect pest except a few. Sun pest damage to wheat crops is recorded every year in some parts of Uzbekistan. Cereal leaf beetles and aphids can also cause economic losses in some years. Russian wheat aphid, locust, and grasshoppers also become a problem for wheat crops sporadically. Most of the insect pest damage to the wheat crops occurs in spring. Methods of insect pest control Resistant varieties Insect pest resistant varieties offer the most effective, economically sustainable, and environmentally friendly approach to addressing the problem. However, the present commercial wheat varieties in Uzbekistan are susceptible to the prevalent insect pests. Efforts are being undertaken to identify insect pest resistant varieties through international collaborations. Chemical control At present, wheat insect pests are controlled by spraying suitable chemicals available in the local market. Only the Sun pest is controlled by chemical spray due to substantial economic damage caused by this pest. Aphids are rarely controlled using pesticides, Integrated pest management (IPM) Landis et al (2016; https://doi.org/10.1093/jipm/pmw010) outlined the possibility of using IPM in managing Sun pest together with some other insect pests and yellow rust of wheat in Central Asia. This approach, not being used at present, offers promise in future for managing multiple diseases and pests. Smallholders can benefit more than large wheat producers. The grains easily separate from the spike upon rolling between the palms. A delay in harvesting can result in losses due to shattering of grains; some varieties are susceptible to shattering. Any delay in harvesting may result in lodging of dry wheat plants due to gusty winds. An extended delay in harvesting may negatively affect the quality of grains. A rain-fed wheat crop can mature to the harvest stage much earlier than the irrigated crop. The use of a combine harvester is much more efficient enabling harvesting and threshing simultaneously. Harvesting on smallholders’ wheat fields is done with a hand tools. Drier grains can be stored well until the next crop year. Grains should be stored in proper containers depending on the amount. Commonly used containers include sacks, pits, bins, or concrete structures.
