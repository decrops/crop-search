Introduction The world is moving toward precision agriculture (PA) applications to safeguard the environment and food security, and site-specific soil compaction management is an important branch of PA. Soil compaction (SC) caused by heavy machinery, livestock traffic, and other human activities, as well as by natural phenomena, has a major impact on agriculture. SC forces air out of the pore spaces between soil particles (British Columbia Ministry of Agriculture, 2015; Cambi et al., 2015; Hamza et al., 2005), making it difficult for the crop roots to access water, oxygen, and nutrients, and thereby inhibiting crop development (Bengough et al., 2006; Peralta Ogorek et al., 2024). One of the most widely used indicators of soil compaction is bulk density (BD), calculated as mass per unit volume. As compaction presses soil particles closer together, BD increases; thus, the more compacted a soil is, the higher its BD (FAO, 2023; Frene et al., 2024; Peralta Ogorek et al., 2024). Site-specific SC management, a sub-branch of PA, involves precisely identifying and mapping areas within a field that have different levels of SC. This approach enables farmers to address compaction issues by targeting specific zones with tailored tillage practices collectively known as precision tillage (PT). By replacing uniform, whole-field tillage with zone-specific tillage, PT optimizes crop yields while reducing resource use and minimizing environmental impacts (Corwin, 2013; Reetz, 2016). To clarify the impact of SC levels on crops and their ecosystems, this study investigated the effects of different initial SC levels—manually adjusted and quantified by BD measurements—on the soil physical and chemical properties, crop development, and nutrient access and uptake in a spring wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) field, and their influence on crop yield. Wheat was selected because it is the second most consumed crop globally, and thus improvements and its yield would positively impact global food security (Reynolds et al., 2022). Previous studies (Batey, 2009; British Columbia Ministry of Agriculture, 2015; Kopittke et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2024) indicate that SC is a significant issue across various agricultural regions and can cause substantial yield deficits in high-input farming systems. Furthermore, SC is considered responsible for crop yield reductions of approximately 6% to as much as 50% worldwide, depending on the region and farming practices involved (Jamali et al., 2021; Shaheb et al., 2021). Most research in this area has focused on the effects of SC on crop productivity (Liu et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024), while overall nutrient dynamics have received comparatively little attention. Notable exceptions include recent investigations analyzing nutrient deficiencies (Sun et al., 2024) and root growth responses (Yu et al., 2024), as well as earlier studies examining the effects of SC on overall nutrient accessibility (Ishaq et al., 2001; Islam et al., 2014). Despite the relative scarcity of research, understanding nutrient accessibility under varying SC levels is crucial for optimizing crop yields and improving sustainable agricultural practices, even if the complexity of soil–plant interactions makes such analyses challenging. Soil compaction often hinders root penetration and impedes the microbial activity needed to break down organic matter, making if difficult for plants to access water and nutrients (Wu et al., 2024). Although various methods have been employed to study soil–plant interactions, such as root growth imaging, nutrient uptake assessment through tissue analysis, yield–nutrient relationship studies, and weather impact correlations, these approaches often fail to account for spatial variability in SC and its influence on nutrient dynamics. There is a need for an integrated model that accurately predicts yield variations across different SC levels. Therefore, this study develops an integrated framework based on soil nutrient management maps, which account for both the effects of SC on nutrient utilization efficiency and the spatial variability in SC. To build this framework, the study examined how initial SC variations—expressed as differences in BD—affected soil nutrient accessibility, soil and growth properties, and yield in wheat crops. Ground truth data (GTD) collected at various growth stages provided insights into soil–plant interactions, which were then used to develop the soil nutrient management maps. These maps provide insights into nutrient dynamics under varying SC conditions and guide precision tillage and fertilization strategies. 2. Materials and methods 2.1. Initial experimental setup 2.1.1. Study site selection The study field was located at the Hokkaido University experimental farm in the central-western part of the Sapporo campus. The field selection was carefully planned based on the agricultural practices of the previous season and the scheduled activities for the next season, as coordinated by the farm technicians. Figure 1 illustrates the experimental location. The dimensions of the study field, (17 × 18.9) m, were selected to minimize the effect of variations in infield climate and soil properties. In this way, any variation in crop development would be clearly attributable to the differences in SC treatment. 2.1.2. Field preparation and sowing The experimental field was prepared as follows. After marking the perimeter, the field was plowed with a rotary plow, and then given two passes with a rotary harrower. ANordsten row planter with a 2.5m width (Kongskilde Industries A/S, Denmark) was calibrated to ensure accurate seed and fertilizer application. After calibration, the sowing was performed. Then, following the standard practice of wheat sowing in the Hokkaido region, the field was rolled once with a Cambridge roller (SUGANO FARM MACHINERY MFG. Co., Ltd., Japan). This step enables firm root-system development in wheat crops. Additionally, it limits the evaporation of soil moisture and, thus, is crucial for the growth of wheat (Dierauer, 2017). Table 1 contains the technical data of the machinery involved in the sowing and the initial soil conditions measured through standard laboratory processes. For the plot compaction treatment, a 400kg vibratory plate compactor with 6.8kW of power (BOMAG mbH, Germany) was used. Figure 1 illustrates the 5 different levels of BD associated with the various SC treatments. The vibratory plate compactor passed 4, 3, 2, or 1 time(s) over the respective subplots. This field-based approach captured the soil’s actual response to mechanical loading, which cannot be adequately replicated in computer simulations. The controlled compaction methodology in this study was designed to realistically simulate SC conditions resulting from farm machinery traffic. respectively. These incremental changes closely mirror field observations under typical machinery traffic. This non-linear response aligns with the literature, which shows that the greatest compaction occurs in the first few passes, with diminishing effects thereafter (Hamza et al., 2005; Nawaz et al., 2013). Our single-season design captures a comprehensive range of compaction levels, reflecting the spectrum typically seen over multiple seasons, thus providing robust and field-relevant data on soil–plant interactions across compaction gradients. As shown in Fig. 1, the study field had two 1.7m tractor traffic paths and two 0.5m walking paths for easy management and application of pesticides and herbicides without disturbing the SC treatments. The traffic paths are represented as vertical gaps in the figure, while the walking paths are indicated by the horizontal gaps and the vertical gap in the center between the colored rectangles. This is because wheat crops in the Hokkaido region take 6 to 10 days to fully emerge (Iwama, 2009; NARO, 2017). The number of seedling rows that emerged on each subplot was counted. Rows with at least three visible seedlings above the soil were counted as an emerged row. This measurement was carried out daily until no more rows were emerging. This was 17 days after planting, and the results are plotted in Fig. 2 (b). There were approximately 20 rows per subplot, and by the 12th day after planting, the Control subplots and the BD4 subplots had at least 3 seedlings emerging in every row, while the BD3 and BD2 subplots took one extra day to show similar results. All BD1 subplots required about 15 days after planting for all the rows to emerge. The graph in Fig. 2 (b) clarifies the developmental status of crops on each subplot by showing the average number of crops per m2 for that subplot. Soil physical properties The initial soil BD, moisture content, organic matter, soil texture, and cone index readings were conducted between the field preparation and planting period. These results are included in Table 1, and the measurements were made according to the methods described in previous research (Motsara et al., 2008; Amanor et al., 2024). In addition, we conducted additional soil sampling 30 days after the SC treatment described in Section 2.1.2. The sampling depth was 0 to 0.3m, and soil was collected at three depth ranges (0 to 0.1m, 0.1 to 0.2m, and 0.2 to 0.3m) using soil ring samplers. This specific depth was chosen because about 70% of crop root biomass is within this depth range (Jackson et al., 1996). The extracted samples were used to determine the BD variation in the various subplots. These measured BD values for each subplot served as the baseline indicators for SC conditions. Soil chemical properties and crop tissue nutrient content For this experiment, we considered soil phosphorus (P), potassium (K), calcium (Ca), and magnesium (Mg) content, in addition to pH and cation exchange capacity (CEC), as the soil chemical properties of interest, in line with the wheat growth guidelines in previous studies (Paulsen et al., 1997; Jones, 2015; Gelderman et al., 2019; Wise et al., 2024). We also considered the total nitrogen (Total-N), P, K, Ca, and Mg content when measuring the absorbed macronutrient levels in crop biomass. Soil samples from 0 to 0.3m depth and crop biomass samples were collected at the Heading/beginning of flowering stage, which is a critical growth stage for wheat based on both the trend in SPAD values depicted in Fig. 5, and the cumulative nutrient uptakes reported in previous studies (Paulsen et al., 1997; Jones, 2015). We prepared 200g of soil samples and 150g of crop biomass samples for each of the five groups of subplots (BD1–BD4 and the Control) and sent them to a laboratory at the Chemical Research Institute of the Tokachi Federation of Agricultural Cooperatives (TFAC), Obihiro, Hokkaido, Japan, for analysis. P, K, Mg, and Ca were measured in their oxide forms—i.e., P2O5, K2O, MgO, and CaO, respectively—which are the forms in which they become accessible to crops (Paulsen et al., 1997; Pandey et al., 2020). The reporting of P, K, Mg, and Ca as their oxide forms in soil testing is rooted in historical chemistry practices and fertilizer Fig. 5 Measured SPAD trend in crop leavesIshmael Nartey AMANOR et al. :Assessing wheat yield response to soil compaction using machine learning —Across-validated study of soil and nutrient dynamics for precision agriculture — 267 industry conventions. Thus, most wet chemistry laboratory methods convert nutrients into oxides during testing. This practice ensures that soil test results can be directly compared with fertilizer grades, which are typically expressed as %N-%P2O5-%K2O, and contributes to the global standardization of nutrient content. Soil test results using nutrient oxide forms allow for direct and easy comparison with fertilizer recommendations for farmers (Sandhya et al., 2023; Achmad et al., 2024). A critical review of three studies on wheat growing (Jones, 2015; Gelderman et al., 2019; Wise et al., 2024) suggested that mid-season soil sampling during the heading to flowering stages, which corresponds to Feekes stages 9 to 10.5, is effective for detecting nutrient deficiencies in wheat. During this critical growth stage, wheat plants experience rapid development and increased nutrient requirements. More than 70% of the total above-ground N and P accumulate by the end of the heading and flowering stages, while 55% of grain N and P come from the redistribution of these accumulated nutrients. During Milk ripening, lower leaves may die because nutrients stored in the lower leaves move to the grain. This phenomenon of critical growth stage was evident in Fig. 5, where the highest SPAD readings were recorded at the Heading/beginning of flowering growth stage, after which the level dropped below the levels recorded in the first two growth stages. The increasing SPAD values from the Tillering stage through the Heading/beginning of flowering (Fig. 5) indicate robust chlorophyll accumulation as the crop increases its photosynthetic capacity. The maximum SPAD value at the Heading/beginning of flowering represents optimal chlorophyll content, coinciding with the crop’s transition from vegetative to reproductive growth. The substantial decrease in SPAD readings during Milk formation and Milk ripening (Fig. 5) reflects the natural senescence process. During Milk formation, wheat plants remobilize nutrients from vegetative tissues to developing grains, leading to chlorophyll degradation and reduced SPAD values. The trends in Fig. 5 demonstrate efficient chlorophyll dynamics and nutrient remobilization from vegetative to reproductive structures, which is a key trait for grain yield. The steep decline of approximately 50% from the Heading/beginning of flowering stage to the Milk ripening stage highlights the significant nutrient translocation to the developing grains. Sensor calibration and usage To reduce the time-consuming process of repeated sampling and laboratory analyses, we used the seven-in-one soil sensor mentioned in Section 2.2.1. for rapid measurement of soil properties. The sensor’s parameters and error margins are available in the sensor’s manual (JXCT, 2020). After evaluation, we identified the need for calibration for certain properties. The pH and soil temperature sensing had acceptable margins of error, while the P, K, N, and moisture content measurements were inaccurate. For this study, we further calibrated the sensor to measure soil volumetric moisture content, moisture content dry basis, and moisture content wet basis. We started by drying 1.5 kg of agricultural soil using the oven drying method (Motsara et al., 2008). To ensure a standardized method for measuring soil volume, three clean graduated cylinders were filled with dried soil. The cylinders were tapped three times from a height of 0.1 m to facilitate natural particle settling. The sensor’s 1-second response time provides adequate resolution for monitoring changes in typical soil properties. With further software development, the sensor could enable real-time data logging and improve temporal coverage, which would be particularly useful for critical activities such as irrigation monitoring. In addition, its portability offers significant advantages for spatial mapping campaigns, allowing comprehensive site characterization across multiple locations, while fixed deployment supports long-term monitoring of soil dynamics at key positions. To ensure measurement accuracy, it is important to maintain consistent insertion depth and firm soil contact. Regular seasonal calibration using laboratory-standard procedures, along with data validation to identify outliers or drift, will further enhance measurement precision. When batch sampling and data management are necessary, optimizing scheduled sampling intervals to align with soil response times and environmental conditions is recommended. Finally, strategic deployment planning that accounts for soil heterogeneity, seasonal variations, and site accessibility will maximize both data quality and research outcomes. 2.3. Crop yield (CY) was calculated in t/ha by harvesting a one-meter square area of the wheat from each subplot and applying Eq. (10) (Martin et al., 2011). Conclusions This study examined the effects of SC on soil physical and chemical properties. All these properties were analyzed to determine their relation, if any, to the final wheat yield. Among the 26 properties measured, 3 properties, all of them soil chemical properties, and their discrete responses to BD treatment levels were found to reflect variations in the final yield. The combination of soil P, Ca, and Mg levels allowed us to estimate yield with an approximately 14% improvement in the coefficient of determination (R2 = 0.9720) compared to the best single property estimation (R2 = 0.8528). Additionally, the final yields recorded in the soft subplots, Control and BD4, were more than 100% higher than those in the hardest subplot, BD1. Results from this research suggest that, when it comes to spring wheat, the soil needs a certain level of SC—about 1.24Mg/m3 —to produce an optimum yield. These results suggest that soil amendments such as tillage affect the final crop yield, and as such, the level of SC needs to be managed to suit the specific requirements of the crop being cultivated. This is why the two hardest plots, BD1 and BD2, had low moisture at the root zones and less access to available soil nutrients, thus resulting in a low final yield. This supports the logic behind site-specific SC, termed PT, which is to apply the required depth of tillage to specific regions where it is required, and our research results demonstrate why this PT concept is important. Insights from this research were used to develop farm input maps, which could help agriculturalists understand the nutrient content remaining in the soil after the crop utilizes its share. Our experimental focused on a single cause of SC—namely, soil densification by heavy farm machinery. This particular SC mechanism reduces the pore spaces in the soil structure without breaking the soil particles into smaller pieces and affecting their textural structure. Hence, the outcome of this research is restricted to this specific concept of compaction. Although our results are limited to specific experimental conditions, the developed models and outcomes provide a blueprint for adaptation across various geographical regions, climates, soil types, crop types, and longer-term management situations. Further research using different crops will be needed to determine their responses to BD treatment as well.
