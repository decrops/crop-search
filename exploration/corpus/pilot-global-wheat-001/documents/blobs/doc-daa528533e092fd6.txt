The possible growth and production of wheat have been negatively influenced by salinity stress, which is a critical environmental factor. Therefore, our study aimed to determine the salinity tolerance threshold of two wheat cultivars (Amin and Barzegar) via photochemical efficiency and ion homeostasis under eight salinity levels (0.5, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 14 dS m −1 ) using a completely randomized design with three replicates. Both wheat cultivars experienced a decrease in the investigated leaf area from the 2 dS m −1 salinity level, with the shoot and root dry weight trends remaining unchanged until the 6 dS m −1 level, followed by a change in the shoot and root dry weight trends. Some significant interactions between salinity stress and cultivars were found in photosynthetic pigments and chlorophyll fluorescence parameters. Increasing salinity stress reduced the F v /F m , F v /F 0 and YPII in both wheat cultivars. However, a significant increase in non-photochemical quenching (NPQ) was observed as salinity intensity increased. The increase in NPQ was approximately 30% lower in Barzegar than that in Amin under severe stress. The antioxidant enzyme activity, K + concentration, and K + /Na + ratio in the cultivar Barzegar were remarkably higher than those in Amin; meanwhile, Amin maintained a higher Na + concentration under salinity stress. Accordingly, photosynthesis parameters, redox, and ionic homeostasis were observed to be linked with salinity tolerance, and Amin and Barzegar were recognized as the sensitive (salt’s tolerance threshold: 3.67 dS m −1 ) and tolerant (salt’s tolerance threshold: 7.13 dS m −1 ) cultivars, respectively. Therefore, some wheat cultivars, such as Barzegar, could manage environmental stress by improving antioxidant enzyme activities, K + concentration, and the K + /Na + ratio to maintain a higher tolerance threshold when salinity stress is raised.

Soil and water quality affect crop growth and yield in arid and semi-arid areas where crops are subjected to the accumulation of dissolved salts (Azeem et al. 2023 ). This phenomenon is also known as salinity stress, which is an important abiotic stress that leads to yield loss (Maryum et al. 2022 ). Salinity stress responses are complex. It is well known that salinity impairs numerous physiological, biochemical and molecular functions (Seleiman et al. 2022 ). It also causes osmotic and ionic stress, leading to oxidative stress (Maryum et al. 2022 ). Moreover, photosynthesis decreases because of the reduced leaf area, increased chlorophyllase and chlorophyll degradation, decreased stomatal conductance and CO 2 uptake, and decreased net photosynthetic assimilation, all of which reduce crop growth and development (Wungrampha et al. 2018 ). Ekim et al. ( 2024 ) reported that under salinity stress, gas exchange in the stomata and the highest quantum yield related to PSII (F v /F m ) may be suppressed, causing growth reduction. Most detrimental effects of salinity are associated with nutrient uptake by crops. Recent research has indicated that the reduction in nutrient uptake under salinity stress can be ascribed to changes in Na + and K + transporters (Ali et al. 2021 ). El-Hawary et al. ( 2023 ) also reported a reduction in K + content and K + /Na + ratio in wheat exposed to salinity stress, while Saddiq et al. ( 2019 ) showed that an inverse relationship exists between Na + and K + ions. An early response to salinity is an improvement in antioxidant enzyme activities (Shah et al. 2021 ), which allows crops to protect their cells from damage by reactive oxygen species, leading to increased stress tolerance (Shah et al. 2021 ). Alinia et al. ( 2024 ) found that salinity stress affected the shoot dry weight of Cyamopsis tetragonoloba L. by changing chlorophyll fluorescence parameters and reducing photosynthetic pigments, membrane stability index, and relative water content. A study on Phaseolus vulgaris L. also showed that salinity stress can significantly reduce chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, carotenoids, and photosynthetic activity (Alinia et al. 2022 a).

The responses of crops to salinity differ considerably at multiple organizational levels, depending on the possible type, species, plant growth stage and duration, and stress intensity (Hameed et al. 2021 ). Wheat, a major winter crop (Mazzilli et al. 2016 ) has exhibits moderate salinity tolerance (Quamruzzaman et al. 2022 ). Recent studies have indicated that differences between wheat cultivars may occur in response to salinity stress. The responses of wheat cultivars to salinity stress can be recognized by diverse morpho-physiological and biochemical changes (Ayaz et al. 2022 ; Talaat and Hanafy 2022 ; Masarmi et al. 2023 ). Therefore, the present investigation evaluated the salinity tolerance threshold of two wheat cultivars via morpho-physiological and biochemical responses to assess their performance and reliability, and to find the appropriate ones to assess the salinity tolerance threshold. We hypothesized that the cultivar Barzegar could alleviate salinity injury and enhance photosynthetic pigments and chlorophyll fluorescence parameters by improving antioxidant enzyme activity, K + concentration, and the K + /Na + ratio.

The current study was organized at the School of Agriculture, Shiraz University, in February 2022, as a factorial experiment using a completely randomized design. The wheat cultivars (Amin and Barzegar) used in this experiment, Amin and Barzegar, were provided by the Seed and Plant Improvement Institute (Karaj, Iran). Seven seeds were sown in each plastic pots (20 × 18 cm, inner diameter × depth) filled with 4.5 kg of soil and placed in a greenhouse at a temperature of 29 ± 2 ◦ C, RH of 80%, and a natural photoperiod. The soil was a field mix (depth of 0–30 cm) collected at the Campus of Shiraz University. The physicochemical properties of the soils are presented in Table 1 . After emergence, the plants were thinned, leaving five seedlings in each pot; 13 days after sowing (DAS) (Zadoks 13: emergence of the third leaf), salinity treatments of 0.5 (control), 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 14 dS m − 1 (corresponding to 0, 0.96, 2.24, 3.52, 4.8, 6.08, 7.36, and 8.64 g L − 1 NaCl in tap water, respectively). In this study, the concentration of NaCl solution in the pots was increased gradually from 2.5 to 14.5 dS m − 1 . This gradual increment in concentration was performed to minimize osmotic shock and root damage and to allow the plants to adapt gradually to salt stress. The electrical conductivity of the soil was investigated using a pre-calibrated EC meter (HI 2315, HANNA Instruments, Ltd, USA) to control salinity.

At the end of the growth stage (63 days after sowing), all plants were harvested, and growth parameters, such as leaf area, were measured using a leaf area meter (Delta‑T Device). Shoot and root dry weights were measured after drying the plants at 70 °C until they reached a constant weight.

Samples of leaf tissues were taken at 62 DAS (Zadoks 47: Flag leaf sheath opening) from 10:00 AM to 12:00 AM, immediately frozen in liquid N 2 and stored at − 80 ◦ C until further use. Chlorophyll a, b, and carotenoid contents were measured in samples of 1 g following homogenization in 5 mL of 80% (v/v) acetone and 0.5 g of magnesium carbonate in the dark by applying a pre-chilled mortar and, mortar. The homogenate was centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 15 min at room temperature (RT), and the OD 663 , OD 645 and OD 470 were measured spectrophotometrically (7315 UV/VIS, Jenway, Staffordshire, UK). Fully expanded uppermost leaves were sampled at 62 DAS (Zadoks 47: flag leaf sheath opening) in three replicates to measure antioxidant enzyme activities. A leaf sample of 0.1 g homogenized in a mortar and pestle with three mM of the extraction buffer containing 1% (w/v) PVP and 0.1 mM EDTA in potassium-phosphate buffer (100 mM, pH 7.0); The supernatant contributed to the peroxidase (POD) enzyme assays in a solution that contained 2 mL potassium-phosphate buffer (60 mM, pH 6.1), 0.5 mL guaiacol (28 mM) and 100 µL of sample, following the 0.5 mL addition of H 2 O 2 (5 mM) and OD 470 (Alinia et al. 2022 a). These values were determined spectrophotometrically (7315 UV/VIS, Jenway).

Three milliliters of extraction buffer, 75 µL of NBT, 4 µM riboflavin, 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.5), 13 mM methionine and, 0.1 mM EDTA were used to measure superoxide dismutase (SOD) at OD 560 . (Beauchamp and Fridovich 1971 ).

Catalase (CAT) activity was assayed using the method of Dazy et al. ( 2008 ) at OD 240, using a solution of 30 mM H 2 O 2 and 3 mM potassium phosphate buffer (50 mM, pH 7.0) mixed with 100 µL of the sample.

Ascorbate peroxidase (APX) activity was determined following the method of Nakano and Asada ( 1981 ) in a solution of 2.490 mL potassium phosphate buffer (50 mM, pH 7.0), 30 µL EDTA (0.1 mM), 300 µL ascorbate (0.5 mM, AsA), 30 µL H 2 O 2 (30 mM), and 150 µL of sample at OD 290 .

Oven-dried shoot samples (0.5 g) were ground into a fine powder and then ashed in an electric oven (at 450 ◦ C temperature for 4 h) followed by digestion in 1 N HCl. The K + and Na + concentrations were determined using flame photometry (Ali et al. 2021 ).

Fresh leaf samples of 10 mm diameter were weighed (FW) and subsequently immersed in distilled water for 6 h to achieve full turgidity, and the turgid weight (TW) was measured. The samples were then dried at 85 °C and their weight (DW) was observed.

The salinity tolerance threshold (EC t ) and other parameters were determined based on shoot dry weight (B) using the Alinia et al. ( 2021 ) procedure.

Statistical analyses were performed using SAS software version 9.1. The means were compared using the least significant difference (LSD) test ( p < 0.05). Heat map correlation analysis and principal component analysis (PCA) were performed using Origin 2019 (Origin Lab Corp, USA).

In this study, salinity stress affected the morphophysiological and biochemical parameters of the two wheat cultivars investigated. We also evaluated the salinity tolerance threshold of these wheat cultivars. Salinity stress significantly decreased shoot and root dry weights and leaf area in both cultivars, like reports by Masarmi et al. ( 2023 ) and Kaya et al. ( 2023 ). Higher shoot and root dry weight and leaf area were found in the cultivar Barzegar than in cultivar Amin. Following crop evaluation, Alinia et al. ( 2021 ) detected higher growth in salt-tolerant cultivars under salinity stress because of higher ion homeostasis and photosynthetic efficiency.

In the present study, salinity stress significantly reduced the leaf photosynthetic pigments in both cultivars in a significant manner. Chlorophyll degradation was lower in Barzegar than that in Amin under salinity stress, suggesting that Barzegar has a higher salt tolerance threshold. Furthermore, this is likely related to lower chlorophyllase synthesis in Barzegar as a result of increased antioxidant enzyme activities. According to numerous researchers, lower photosynthetic pigment values are the most common cause of chlorophyll degradation under salinity stress, because salinity induces chlorophyllase gene expression, reduces chlorophyll content, and damages the photosynthetic apparatus (Alamer 2023 ; Sadak et al. 2023 ; Loudari et al. 2023 ).

Salinity stress decreased all chlorophyll fluorescence parameters except NPQ in the investigated cultivars. Reductions in F v /F m , F v /F 0 , and YPII and increases in NPQ occurred under salinity stress. Similarly, Masarmi et al. ( 2023 ) reported that F m , F v , and F v /F 0 decreased, whereas F 0 increased under salinity stress conditions. Under salinity stress conditions, the cultivar Barzegar displayed higher F v /F m , F v /F 0 and YPII, whereas Amin exhibited lower F v /F m , F v /F 0 and YPII. The lower intensity of photoinhibition, damage to reaction center II, higher absorption of light energy in PSII, and rate of electron transport in Barzegar could be related to a higher salt tolerance threshold and redox and ion homeostasis under salinity stress, whereas Amin is sensitive to lower ion homeostasis. Lima et al. ( 2018 ) also reported that F v /F m indicates photoinhibition or stress conditions in the PSII.

Considerable changes were recorded in antioxidant enzyme activities, the highest of which was recorded in Barzegar. With increasing salinity levels, SOD, CAT, and APX decreased owing to oxidative stress; POD was increased to detoxify ROS in both wheat cultivars. Similarly, Maslennikova et al. ( 2023 ) observed a higher POD activity under salinity stress. In contrast, Munawar et al. ( 2021 ) found lower antioxidant enzyme activity in some genotypes. This can be ascribed to the higher production of reactive oxygen species and oxidative stress. Several studies have investigated salt tolerance mechanisms in different crops by improving antioxidant defenses to reducing reactive oxygen species (Pakar et al. 2016 ; Pirasteh-Anosheh and Emam 2018 ; Alinia et al. 2022 a, b, 2024 ; Bakyani et al. 2022 ; Pirasteh-Anosheh et al. 2023 ;).

With increasing salinity levels, the potassium concentration and potassium/sodium ratio decreased, whereas sodium concentration increased in both wheat cultivars. K is an important element in plant growth and productivity. However, under salinity stress conditions, the uptake of such elements may be decreased, which could exert harmful effects on stress tolerance. This decrease is caused by the promotion of sodium uptake, which leads to the activation of various enzymes, protein synthesis, and plant photosynthesis. A similar study by Munawar et al. ( 2021 ) observed some significant effects on ionic homeostasis in response to salinity stress. Moreover, Kumar et al. ( 2017 ) showed that K + transporters were improved in the shoots of the salt-tolerant genotype, but reduced in the salt-sensitive genotype. Alinia et al. ( 2022 a, b) also deduced that potassium-activated enzymes regulate osmotic pressure and improve membrane stability.

In this study, salinity stress decreased RWC in both wheat cultivars. The combination of the control and cultivar treatments yielded the best results compared to the other treatments. Salinity stress increases osmotic stress and decreases turgor pressure, thereby reducing water uptake and RWC (Menezes et al. 2017 ). Similarly, Sarker and Oba ( 2020 ) reported that salinity stress has negative effects on plants by decreasing RWC. Negative impacts of salinity stress were observed in A. tricolour varieties, and soil water availability and RWC decreased significantly (Sarker and Oba 2020 ).

According to these results, salinity caused a reduction in the salt tolerance threshold. According to RB and salt tolerance thresholds, the cultivar Amin (3.67 dS m −1 ) is more salt-sensitive than Barzegar (7.13 dS m −1 ). We found a marked positive correlation between Fv/Fm, Fv/F0, YPII, and RB.

Salinity stress significantly decreased growth attributes, increased chlorophyll degradation, and changes in antioxidant enzyme activities in the two investigated wheat cultivars. More noteworthy reductions in shoot and root dry weight, leaf area, and chlorophyll fluorescence parameters (except nonphotochemical quenching) were observed in Amin than in Barzegar. Additionally, higher sodium concentration and lower concentrations of potassium and the potassium/sodium ratio were observed in comparison with the cultivar Barzegar. Furthermore, salinity stress led to a reduction in the relative water content of the wheat cultivars, especially in Amin. The highest peroxidase activity was recorded for the cultivar Barzegar. Furthermore, we observed a higher salt tolerance threshold in Barzegar than in the cultivar Amin. Therefore, photosynthetic efficiency and ion homeostasis could be recognized as suitable parameters for evaluating salinity tolerance in these two wheat cultivars. However, relative water content should not be considered in the salinity stress assessment because it does not have adequate sensitivity to salinity. The findings of the present investigation also provide new evidence for investigating the molecular mechanisms of Barzegar in alleviating salinity injury and improving the tolerance threshold.
