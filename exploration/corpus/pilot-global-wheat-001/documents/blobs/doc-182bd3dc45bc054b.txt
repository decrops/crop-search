As a global alliance unparalleled in the history of wheat research, WHEAT linked advanced sciencewithfield-levelresearchandextension in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). TheCRPworkedtoraisetheproductivity,pro duction, and affordable availability of wheat for 2.5 billion resource-poor consumers who depend on the crop as a staple food. WHEAT partner organizations developed and tested moreproductive,disease-resistantwheatvari eties and efficient, sustainable wheat-based cropping systems. They also collaborated on: • Socioeconomic analysis for greatest value and social inclusion. • Better seed production and distribution systems. • A new generation of trained and inspired wheat professionals. Workwascarriedoutbyacommunityofmore than 200 public and private organizations worldwide,amongthemnationalgovernments, companies,internationalcenters,regionaland local agencies, and farmers. The participation andengagementofregionalandlocalpartners underpinnedthesuccessofWHEAT’sprojects. WHEAT activities and projects were grouped into four flagships. They focused on the LMIC wheat-producingcountriesofEastAsia,South Asia, Central and West Asia and North Africa, sub-Saharan Africa, and Latin America. In2020,one-fifthofallfarmsaroundtheworld grew wheat, though this figure is expected to decline by 4% in 2030. As of 2020, wheat is cultivated on 216 million ha, 29% of which is in lower- and lower-middle income countries. Screening operations in wheat stem rust phenotyping platforms lead to the release of Ug99-resistant wheat cultivars – 2019 After an almost 50-year absence, wheat stem rust, a deadly fungal disease, returned to wheat fields with a vengeance, spreading to East Africa, the Middle East, and Europe. The disease, which can destroy healthy wheat plants just weeks before harvest, was con trolled for decades using rust-resistant wheat varieties developed in the 1950s by CIMMYT scientist and Nobel Prize laureate Dr. Norman E. Borlaug and his colleagues. As part of the BorlaugGlobalRustInitiative (launched in 2008), CIMMYT and Cornell University established the International Stem Rust Phenotyping Platform in Kenya, in collaboration with the Kenya Agricultural and Livestock Research Organization. The platform supports screening against stem rust of up to 50,000 wheat lines per year from up to 25 countries. It also facilitates breeding to identify and develop new resistant lines. Over the last decade, more than 100 wheat cultivars resistant to the stem rust race Ug99 and its variants have been released,10 cgiar | WHEAT Summary Report 2017-2021 benefiting smallholder wheat farmers world wide and demonstrating the progress made in breeding for stem rust resistance globally. Significant genetic gains in grain yield delivered by breeding research – 2020 WHEAT’s Global Wheat Breeding Program (GWP) delivered significant genetic gains to national agricultural research system partners by developing high-yielding, cli mate-resilient, and disease-resistant wheat germplasm. Using a set of previous studies, GWP assessed the performance of bread wheat breeding germplasm developed through the extensive analysis of interna tional data sets in 2020. The results indicated that genetic gains range from 0.5% to 1% per annum, which, on average, represents 46.6 kilograms per hectare per year. In another long-term, on-station experiment, a set of historical varieties representing 50 years of wheat breeding was evaluated under optimally irrigated and drought envi ronments. The study demonstrated that WHEAT’s bread wheat breeding program delivered superior germplasm to target envi ronments. In 2020, these efforts led directly to the release of 39 varieties in 10 countries by national partners. WHEAT and MAIZE science, partner ships, and outreach led to policies for conservation agriculture and nutrient management in South Asia, mitigating climate change and increasing small holder income – 2021 Ongoing efforts by WHEAT, MAIZE, and part ners have generated scientific evidence, built capacity, and contributed to stakeholder outreach and knowledge sharing that has informed policies to encourage wide-scale adoption of emission risk-mitigating prac tices in smallholder wheat- and maize-based cropping systems in South Asia. The adop tion of conservation agriculture on 2 million hectares and precision nutrient management on 20 million hectares could potentially ben efit around 14 million smallholders, produce around 14 million tons of extra grain, and earn US$1.3 billion in additional revenue, while reducing approximately 3.4 million tons of CO2 each year. Below is a selection of key findings from the WHEAT review. Quality of science Over decades, WHEAT catalyzed a global network of research and development that deliveredadisproportionatewealthofoutputs in relation to investment. However, partner ships and WHEAT’s reputation as a reliable partner were vulnerable to funding volatility. By 2019, almost four out of five (79%) of WHEAT’s scientific publications were in the top 50% of journals; 21% of the publications were in the bottom half. Publications in Quartile 1 journals (Q1, top 25%) were world class,andsomepushedscientificboundaries; publications in Q2 journals were sound but often routine work; and publications in Q3 and Q4 journals commonly featured flaws. Importance of outputs and outcomes Between 2017 and 2019, WHEAT mainly achieved its planned outputs and outcomes, and unplanned outcomes. WHEAT did not drop any research lines, and added research priorities on mechanization and soil-borne diseases in 2017. Mechanisms for achieving sustainability As a crop, wheat is bound to be central to global food security in the foreseeable future. WHEAT,asaresearchanddevelopmentagent, hadatrackrecordofdeliveringlocalsolutions with a global perspective. The CAS evaluation noted that opportunities and challenges for thewayaheadinclude:theriskoffragmenting the global breeding program; restrictions to exchanging germplasm and ideas; the oppor tunity to integrate research and development in agronomy; misguided emphasis on minor crops; and CGIAR’s focus on process at the expense of results. The CAS evaluation made ten recommenda tions at the CRP level and the CGIAR system level, which include the following: • Support strategic investment in network development and maintenance. • Investigate the nature of the network using larger samples and complementary metrics beyond authorship to ensure network resilience in response to both random and targeted disruptions. Consider opportu nities for expanding networks beyond current nodes. • Ensure support to modernization of the breedingprocessandintegratedapproaches to sustainable intensification, including mechanization.
