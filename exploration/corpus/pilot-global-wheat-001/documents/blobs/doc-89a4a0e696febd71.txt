The 1970s was a decade of revolution for crop physiology and, in particular, for the physiology of temperate cereals. Up to that time, it had proven difficult to correlate annual crop yield with those factors, notably radiation supply, which were known from controlled experiments to influence photosynthetic performance (Evans et al. 1975). Furthermore, it appeared that the tools of crop growth analysis developed in the field by Watson at Rothamsted had exhausted their usefulness: the product of leaf area index and net assimilation rate rarely gave sensible crop growth rates, particularly at high leaf area indices (Watson et al. 1963; Russell et al. 1989). At the same time, interpretation of the results of laboratory work on the environmental control of photosynthesis, and on the roles of sources and sinks in determining grain growth, posed formidable intellectual challenges, principally because of the plasticity of the cereal plant. Competing teams were able to demonstrate sink or source limitation of photosynthesis and yield almost at will by varying plant material and environmental conditions (Evans 1993).
