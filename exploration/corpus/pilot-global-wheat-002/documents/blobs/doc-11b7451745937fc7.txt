Evaluating the effectiveness of overwintering cover crops in supporting spring field trafficability in Manitoba Advisor: Dr. Afua Mante Excess soil water is typical of soils in Manitoba during the early growing season due to snowmelt and heavy rainfall. Managing soil waterto attain optimalsoil strength forsoil trafficabilityis there fore crucial to ensure timely field operations in the region. Over the years, subsurface drainage has been the most popular approach for the removal of excess soil water. However, this approach is not always feasible due to poor internal drainage and/or compromised soil structure. This study evaluated whether a fall shoulder cover crop could improve spring trafficability. We leveraged a multi-year experiment initiated in fall 2020 near Cartwright, MB (Farming Systems Lab). From fall 2022, fall rye (Secale cereale) has been grown to assess effects on soil water dynamics and the resulting soil strength relative to a no-cover control. The trafficability criterion was volumetric water content ≤ 90% of the lower plastic limit in the 0 – 25 cm layer. Across the monitoring period, measured water contents in both treatments commonly exceeded this threshold; consequently, fall rye did not support trafficability. A 2015–2023 climate analysis for Cartwright and comparative regions in Northwest and Eastern Manitoba showed that fall conditions seldom supported success ful rye establishment: neither four consecutive weeks with mean daily temperature ≥10 °C nor the accumulation of 309 growing degree days before cold hardening were achieved. As a result, rye produced insufficient biomass to meaningfully increase evapotranspiration.iii HYDRUS model simulations indicated that delaying termination to June 20 could create brief windows, up to 21 days, where soil moisture met the trafficability threshold; however, these win dows were highly vulnerable to rewetting from subsequent rainfall events. Overall, fall rye as a standalone strategy was not effective for managing excess soil water under these conditions. These findings highlight the limitations of fall rye as a standalone strategy for managing excess soil water in Manitoba and emphasize the need for integrated approaches to im prove trafficability under challenging soil and climatic conditions.

Soil traf ficability is the capacity of the soil to support the weight of machinery and traction without defor mation of the soil (Earl, 1997). In Manitoba the soils are often wet or waterlogged in spring after snowmelt and rainfall, requiring few weeks for soils to become sufficiently dry to support field operations (Hamza & Anderson, 2005). This negatively impacts the time farmers seed their fields and reduces the short growing season in the region even further. Overwintering cover crops have gained attention as a strategy to improve soil trafficability based on their potential to regulate soil water through evapotranspiration and increase water infiltration and percolation through mainte nance and creation of biopores. Considering Manitoba’s climate, it is imperative to evaluate the potential of overwintering cover crops in promoting soil trafficability. 1.2 Climate and soil formation in Manitoba Manitoba’s landscape is shaped by a climate of extremes, cold winters, fleeting but intense sum mers, and unpredictable water patterns, that significantly impact the land formation processes (Manitoba Agriculture and Resource Development, 2021). Located in the heart of the Canadian Prairies, the province lies between the towering Rocky Mountains to the west, the Canadian Shield to the north and east, and the U.S.-Canada border to the south (Peel et al., 2007). Manitoba, located in the eastern part of Canada’s Prairie Provinces, has a cool and relatively dry climate due to its northern latitude and continental interior position. In contrast to Alberta, which lies directly in the rain shadow of the Rocky Mountains, Manitoba is situated more than 900 km east of the moun-2 tains. Its climate is therefore shaped primarily by its distance from both Pacific and Atlantic mois ture sources rather than direct mountain effects, consistent with descriptions of the Prairie ecozone as cold and dry owing to both latitude and continentality (Anderson & Cerkowniak, 2010). Mani toba has an annual precipitation of 400 - 500 mm with about two-thirds falling during May, June, and July, the most critical growing months for growing crops (Anderson & Cerkowniak, 2010). During winter, snowfall accumulates, and upon melting, the resulting water contributes to runoff and leaching, affecting the properties of the soil (Manitoba Agriculture & Resource Development 2021). Temperature differences in terms of effective growing season degree days (EGDD) range from 1600 in the warmer Red River Valley to about 1000 in cooler uplands such as Riding Mountain and the Manitoba Escarpment, where higher elevation shortens the growing season (Ander son & Cerkowniak, 2010). This variationinclimategives riseto different weatherconditions (Peel et al. 2007), affecting how soils form and develop. These conditions create specific soil zones, where certain types of soils dominate (Brady & Weil, 2016). In the Aspen Parkland (a mix of grasslands and trees), there are Black Chernozems, which are dark, fertile soils (Ander son & Cerkowniak, 2010). These soils form in areas with moderate rainfall and are great for farm ing because they are rich in organic matter (Pennock et al., 2011). In higher, forested areas with more trees, cooler temperatures, and more rain, there are Gray Luvisols (Dyck et al., 2023). These soils form because rainwater washes nutrients deeper into the soil, which makes the surface soil lighter and less fertile compared to Chernozems (Anderson & Cerkowniak, 2010). The presence of a highly porous organic material over these soils often leads to poor internal drainage, with approximately 45% of Manitoba's soils exhibiting this characteristic (Eliers et al., 2002). The poor internal drainage of the soils is aggravated by seasonal changes like spring melt causing surface3 runoff and water accumulation, impacting soil strength, trafficability, and thus influencing the re gion's agricultural productivity and necessitating strategic land management. 1.3 Soil strength Soil strength is the maximum shear stress that the soil can withstand without failing (Duncan et al., 2014). Failureoccurs when thesoil particlesrearrange, slideorroll against oneanother,leading to deformation (Mouazen et al., 2002). This property, also known as soil shear strength, depends on the cohesion and frictional properties of soil particles (Mouazen et al., 2002) . These are gov erned by the Mohr-Coulomb failure theorem, expressed by the equation, τ = σtan(Φ) + c, where τ is the soil shear strength, Φ is the internal angle of friction, σ is the normal stress acting on the soil mass and c is the cohesion existing between the soil particles (Zhang et al., 2000). These soil strength properties are influenced by soil type, soil water content, and organic matter (Ghosh, 2013; (Mosaddeghi et al., 2009) Cohesion represents the soil particles' stickiness and the soil's ability to form aggregates (Mo saddeghi et al., 2009). Soil aggregate formation in soils improves the soil structure (Mosaddeghi et al., 2009). An improved soil structure promotes wide range of pore size distribution, improves hydraulic conductivity of the soil, and increases soil’s resistance to soil deformation (Çerçioğlu et al., 2019). Cohesion increases with an increase in clay content and water content. Due to the small size of clay particles, they exhibit a high external specific surface in which the adjacent particles directly attract each other (Craig, 2004). As the soil water content increases, it attracts and binds the clay particles to each other (Mitchell & Soga, 2005). An increase in organic matter increases the water-holding capacity of the soil (Rawls et al., 2003). Hence, an increase in organic matter increases the cohesion of the soil (Mosaddeghi et al., 2009). However, there is an optimal water4 content beyond which cohesion decreases (Zhang et al., 2000). The optimalwater content is equiv alent to a fraction of the plastic limit of the soil beyond which the solid particles are replaced by the additional water leading to the separation of the clay particles (Mitchell & Soga, 2005). As cohesion decreases and aggregates break down, the soil particles disperse and dislodge and may lead to the formation of a surface seal, a dispersed layer of mud on the soil surface. This mud layer can block the soil macropores in the soil, reducing water infiltration Assouline & Mualem, 2000). Friction is the resistance generated by particle interlocking (Craig, 2004). Coarse textured soils, like sandy soils, have stronger frictional effects due to their larger particle size and fewer adsorbed water films (Mitchell & Soga, 2005). However, as soil water content increases, it lubricates the particles, thereby reducing friction and shear strength (Fredlund et al., 2012). Li et al. (2021) ob served that as the water content in silt gradually increases toward saturation, the soil's shear be haviour changes. Initially, the soil exhibits strain-softening, where it weakens under shear. How ever, as the water content increases further, the soil begins to display strain-hardening, becoming stronger with continued shear, accompanied by a notable decrease in the interface shear strength. 1.4 Impact of soil strength on soil trafficability Due to food security and growing population, there has been a necessity for mechanization to increase crop production. In addition, economic pressures to reduce labour costs in agriculture have further driven the adoption of mechanization. The need for mechanization has led to a sig nificant increase in the weight of machinery for field operations (Schjønning et al., 2015). The typical weight of a combine harvester rose from approximately 4000 kg in the 1950s to more than 35, 800 kg by 2020 (Keller & Or, 2022; Schjønning et al., 2015). The capacity of the soil to with stand stresses from these machineries have become a concern in crop production (Hamza & An derson,2005). The ability ofthe soil to support trafficdepends on thesoil strength (Lebert& Horn,5 1991). The most important factor that affects soil strength and hence, trafficability is soil water content (Horn & Fleige, 2009). Excess soil water content reduces soil strength, which reduces the soil trafficability (Hamza & Anderson, 2005). According to Rab et al. (2005), clay soils may have a soil strength of 0.8 MPa when dry during the summer but may be as low as 100 kPa when wet highlighting the significant influence of water on soil strength. 1.5 Impact of soil trafficability on agricultural productivity Poor soil trafficability has significant consequences, including soil deformation, delayed field op erations, and economic losses (Hamza & Anderson, 2005), all of which negatively affect agricul tural productivity and soil health (Lal, 2015). Soil deformation manifests in various forms including rut formation, puddling, and compaction (Soane & Ouwerkerk, 1994). Rut formation occurs when machinery traffics soft, water-saturated soils, causing depressions or grooves in the soil (Raghavan et al., 1978). These ruts reduce water infiltration due to the smearing of the topsoil (Abu-Hamdeh, 2003) and serve as preferential flow paths during the rainy season, leading to erosion and water ponding (Boardman & Poesen, 2006). Puddling occurs when soil aggregates break down under wet conditions, forming plastic mud (Swanson et al. 1955). This exposes organic matter to microbial decomposition, resulting in soil organic carbon loss (Lal & Shukla, 2004). Soil compaction is another consequence of poor trafficability (Arvidsson & Håkansson, 2014). It refers to the increase in bulk density due to the reduction of air-filled porosity in unsaturated soils (Danielson & Sutherland, 1986). Compacted soils hinder aeration, root development, water infil tration and percolation, and drainage, and pose challenges for field management (Chamen et al., 2003). Heavy machinery with high axle loads contributes significantly to compaction, which can6 persist deep into the subsoil (Håkansson et al., 1988). Compaction can also occur naturally due to soil texture and water content (Brady & Weil, 2008), sodic soil conditions (Rengasamy, 2016), or raindrop impact on exposed soil surfaces, forming a hardened layer when dry (Hillel, 1998). The limitations imposed by poor soil trafficability also impact field operations. Wet soils reduce the window of time available for fieldwork, delaying essential tasks such as tillage and seeding (Siemens et al., 1990). This is especially critical in regions with short growing seasons, like Man itoba, where frost-free days range between 75 and 135 days (Manitoba Agriculture and Resource Development, 2021). When crops are not seeded on time, their growth, maturity, and yields are affected (O'Leary et al., 2016). This delay in seeding may also push back harvesting, as seen in 2016 when excess soil water suspended harvesting across much of the southern grain belt of the Prairie Provinces (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016; Saskatchewan Government, 2017). Similarly, in 2007, excessive spring water in northern, central, and eastern Saskatchewan reduced seeded acreage by 25% (Saskatchewan Government, 2007). The economic implications of poor trafficability are profound. Timeliness costs for planting and harvesting are significant because delays reduce yield potential, lower grain quality, increase me chanical and operational losses, and raise labor and equipment expenses (Siemens et al., 1990; O’Leary et al., 2016; Mosaddeghi et al., 2009; ResearchGate, 2016). Research by Jahun and Iya (2015) on Badala Farm quantified these costs, estimating CAD 5.98/ha/day for planting and CAD 6.02/ha/day for harvesting. Usually, areas with poor trafficability are the most affected during ex treme weather conditions as was the case in Saskatchewan in 2006 where crop insurance claims acres exceeded $61 million (Western Producer, 2006). InManitoba,the impact of extremeweather on crop insurance claims varies significantly between good and bad years. “Good years” are char acterized by low crop insurance claims when yields meet or exceed insured levels, whereas “bad7 years”entailhighindemnitypayoutssuchasthe2021droughtyear,whichtriggeredapproximately $650 million in claims, nearly twice the previous record largely driven by extreme weather-related production shortfalls (Manitoba Co-operator, 2025). In comparison, in 2024, for instance, 9.8 mil lion insured acres had total coverage of $4.66 billion (Manitoba Co-operator, 2024), while in 2025 the total coverage was estimated at $4.2 billion, with an average premium of $13.01 per acre for annual crops (Manitoba Co-operator, 2025). This indicates that years with severe weather result in more affected acres and higher insurance payouts, while favorable conditions help reduce claims and premiums. 1.6 Measuring soil strength for soil trafficability Several methods are used to assess soil strength (Carter, 1990). These methods include determin ing the soil strength parameters, penetration resistance and the plasticity of the soil (Bradford & Gupta, 1986). Some common approaches include use of micro-penetrometer, direct shear test, tri axial text, using a shear vane device and a soil consistency test. • Micro-penetrometer The micro-penetrometer (Figure 1.1) is a miniature soil testing device designed to measure pene tration resistance at a fine scale, making it particularly suitable for assessing soil strength in soft, cohesive soils (Chi & Tessier, 1995). It operates by pushing a small cone or needle into the soil at a controlled rate while continuously recording the resistance force and penetration depth. The re sult is a high-resolution profile (typically millimeter-scale) of resistance versus depth, which re flects changes in soil strength. The setup typically comprises three key components: a load/displacement control and measure ment system—consisting of a control box and control panel along with load and displacement8 transducers; a platen positioning system to hold and adjust the soil sample; and a data-acquisition system that integrates a data logger with a computer interface (Manrique, 2011). During testing, the soil sample is mounted on a platen, and the motor-driven frame applies a con stant displacement rate. A probe connected to the load transducer penetrates the soil, measuring tip resistance, while the displacement transducer tracks penetration depth. These values are used to plot a penetration curve, where higher resistance indicates stronger soil (Wang et al., 2016). Micro-penetrometers are particularly effective for detecting fine-scale heterogeneity in clayey soils, such as structural layering, water variations, and zones of compaction. Research has shown that penetration resistance in cohesive soils drops exponentially with increasing water content, making this method valuable for monitoring strength loss due to rainfall infiltration and assessing spatial variability in the topsoil (Tang et al., 2022). Limitations include challenges with field practicality, as the devices are delicate, require power and precise control, and arethereforebest suitedto small test plots orlaboratory-prepared samples. Another limitation is the restricted volume of soil assessed, since the probe interacts with only a small portion of soil, meaning the results may not represent the bulk strength in heterogeneous field conditions (Chi & Tessier, 1995). In addition, soil constraints can affect performance, as dry or compacted soils may exceed the device’s resistance range, while gravel or organic debris can interfere with accurate readings. Hence, while micro-penetrometers provide highly detailed insight into soil strength dynamics, es pecially in soft, cohesive soils, they are best suited for research applications and less practical for routine field trafficability assessment.9 Figure 1.1 Schematic diagram of the micro-penetrometer equipment (Wang et al., 2016) • Direct shear test The direct shear test (Figure 1.2) is a widely used laboratory method for determining the shear strength parameters of soil, namely cohesion (c) and the internal friction angle (Φ ). In this test, a soil sample, either remolded or undisturbed is placed within a split shear box apparatus. The box is divided into an upper half, which remains stationary, and a lower half, which is moved horizon tally by a motor at a constant speed (Verruijt, 2001; Head & Epps, 1980). A vertical load is first applied to simulate overburden pressure using a loading plate and weights. As the lower half moves, the soil shears along the horizontal plane between the two halves. The resulting horizontal force is measured using a pressure ring or strain gauge and increases until failure occurs (Verruijt, 2001). Shear strength is evaluated by conducting multiple tests at different normal stresses, allow ing for the development of a failure envelope, a plot of shear stress versus normal stress, from which c and Φ are derived. The setup may also include a steel plate to accommodate vertical deformation of the sample during shearing.10 Figure 1.2 Schematic diagram of the direct shear test (Gan et al., 1988) The test can be used for both cohesive and non-cohesive soils. In cohesive soils, it is particularly effective under drained conditions, where pore water pressure can dissipate during the slow shear ing process. For agricultural applications, especially in dry to moderately moist clayey soils, the test offers a relatively simple and quick estimate of shear strength, important for evaluating traffi cability. In some cases, field versions of direct shear devices have been attempted (Ly & Pham, 2020), though most applications are still lab-based. The limitations of the direct shear test are several. First, the predetermined shear plane constrains failureto occur along ahorizontal surface, which maynot correspond to theweakest natural failure surface in the field, leading to a possible overestimation of soil strength. Stress distribution within the shear box is also non-uniform, as friction along the box edges can create stress concentrations that skew results, particularly in cohesive or structured soils (Kouakou et al., 2020). Another draw back is the lack of pore pressure measurement in standard setups, which restricts the test to total stress analysis. This makes it unsuitable for accuratelysimulating undrained shear conditions, such11 as those occurring during rapid wheel loading. In addition, the requirement for small, trimmed samples (typically 6–10 cm) can disturb the natural soil structure, limiting representation of larger aggregates and field heterogeneity. Finally, the test has limited field relevance because it provides only a single shear event under controlled loading, without capturing the effects of dynamic, re peated loads, rutting, or cumulative soil damage that occur during real field traffic (Rochelle, 1981).The direct shear test remains a practical and efficient method to obtain basic shear strength parameters in cohesive soils, especially under controlled lab conditions. However, its inherent simplifications, especially regarding failure plane control and pore pressure exclusion mean it should be used cautiously when interpreting soil trafficability in variable, real-world field condi tions. • Triaxial test The triaxial shear test (Figure 1.3) is a highly versatile and comprehensive laboratory method used toevaluatetheshearstrengthofsoils,particularlycohesivesoilslikeclays.Inthistest,acylindrical soil specimen (typically 3.5 – 7 cm in diameter and 7 – 14 cm in height) is encased in a rubber membrane and placed in a triaxial cell filled with water. Circular plates and watertight connections ensure the sample is uniformly confined. A controlled confining pressure is applied via the water around the sample, simulating in-situ stress conditions, while an axial load is applied from above using a steel rod driven by an electric motor. The resulting vertical deformation is measured until the sample fails in shear, indicated by a peak in force or visible deformation (Head & Epps, 1980; Verruijt, 2001). There are three common triaxial test types, each designed to simulate different field conditions (Lade, 2016). In the Unconsolidated Undrained (UU) test, no drainage is allowed and the sample is quickly loaded, simulating rapid loading events such as a wheel pass over saturated soil, with12 results reflecting total shear strength. In the Consolidated Undrained (CU) test, the soil is first allowed to consolidate under confining pressure with drainage, after which it is sheared without further drainage; pore water pressure is measured during shearing, enabling the calculation of ef fective stress parameters. Finally, in the Consolidated Drained (CD) test, the sample consolidates and is sheared slowly under conditions that allow full drainage, making it suitable for evaluating long-term loading or partially saturated field conditions. Unlike the direct shear test, the failure plane in triaxial testing forms naturally, often along an inclined plane, resulting in a more representative failure mechanism. By performing the test at different confining pressures, a family of Mohr circles is generated, allowing for the derivation of cohesion (c) and friction angle (Φ ) (Craig & Knappett, 2012). Triaxial testing is especially valuable for saturated cohesive soils, as it can simulate both drained and undrained loading conditions, critical forunderstanding trafficabilityin wet agricultural fields. For example, the UU test provides a direct measure of undrained shear strength (su), which deter mines whether the soil can withstand rapid loading by machinery without deformation. The flexi bility of test modes allows the characterization of both short-term (undrained) and long-term (drained) strength behavior in cohesive soils, which is essential for evaluating soil strength and rutting risk under varying water conditions (Holtz et al., 2011). Limitations include the method’s laboratory-intensive nature—requiring specialized equipment, meticulous sample preparation, and trained personnel—making it time-consuming and costly; the difficulty of obtaining high-quality, undisturbed samples from structured agricultural soils, where any disturbance can alter measured behavior; significant time requirements (consolidated-drained tests may take days to weeks to ensure full drainage, and even CU/UU tests involve multiple steps with detailed pore-pressure monitoring); scale and boundary effects, since small specimens may13 not capture field-scale heterogeneity (fissures, aggregates, layering) and do not replicate repetitive traffic loading; and data-interpretation challenges, because translating laboratory strength param eters into field trafficability still requires modelling or empirical correlations—there is no direct pass/fail threshold for traffic decisions (Budhu, 2015). The triaxial test offers the most robust and informative laboratory assessment of soil shear strength for cohesive soils. It accurately simulates in-situ stress conditions and drainage behavior, making it a key reference in soil mechanics. However, its complexity, time demands, and lab-dependence limit its direct application for rapid field trafficability assessments. Instead, it serves as a founda tion for setting guidelines or calibrating simpler field tools (Craig & Knappett, 2012). The torque required to shear the soil along a cylindrical surface sur rounding the vane is recorded, from which undrained shear strength is calculated based on vane dimensions (Ekwue et al., 2014). Vanes typically have a length-to-diameter ratio of about 2 (e.g., 75 mm high by 37.5 mm radius), though sizes can be adjusted for different soil strengths. The test can be performed manually at shallow depths using handheld devices or at greater depths using motorized systems in boreholes. During testing, the rotation continues until the pointer (or torque gauge) stops increasing, indicat ing soil failure. The vane shear test is specifically designed for saturated, fine-grained cohesive soils such as clays. It is particularly useful for assessing trafficability in wet field conditions because it directly measures the undrained shear strength, which governs a soil's capacity to resist deformation under rapid loading (e.g., by machinery). It can also provide peak and remolded strengths, enabling eval uation of soil sensitivity - how much strength is lost after disturbance. This is critical for traffica bility since sensitive clays can appear firm initially but lose strength rapidly after being disturbed by a vehicle pass (Shen et al., 2024). Limitations of the vane shear test include its applicability primarily to cohesive soils, as it is un suitable for soils with high sand, silt, gravel, or organic matter, which do not fail cleanly around the vane and therefore produce inaccurate or erratic readings. The method is also constrained by a limited strength range: while effective in soft clays, it becomes unreliable in stiff or over-consoli dated clays (typically >100–150 kPa su) where failure does not occur along the ideal cylindrical surface. Depth restrictions further limit its use, as the test is most commonly applied within the top 15–30 cm, making it useful for evaluating topsoil trafficability but requiring additional equipment,15 such as rods or boreholes, for deeper measurements, and it does not provide a continuous strength profile. Rod friction can also introduce errors in manual setups, since friction along the turning rod may inflate torque readings and lead to overestimation of strength, although advanced systems mitigate this by placing sensors at the vane itself. Another limitation is that the test produces only a single parameter output—the undrained shear strength—without distinguishing between cohe sion and friction or simulating drained behavior, which reduces its usefulness for long-term or dry condition assessments. Finally, environmental constraints restrict its application in frozen or very dry soils, though such conditions are less relevant in the context of agricultural trafficability eval uations (Shen et al., 2024). Despite the outlined limitations, the vane shear test offers a quick, practical, and semi-quantitative method to assess undrained shear strength in cohesive soils. It is especially useful for on-site de cisions about machinery access during wet conditions. Its simplicity and relevance to short-term soil strength make it a valuable tool in both construction and agricultural contexts. These range from purely qualitative hand feel methods to basic penetrometer de vices. Such methods emphasize ease of use, though each comes with limitations in accuracy and depth. Below is a comparison of some common simple field methods. Hand Feel and Visual Tests: The simplest “instrument” for soil strength is the farmer’s own hands and feet. For example, squeezing a clod or pressing a thumb into the soil can indicate its firmness. Walking on the soil and observing if footprints sink in or if the soil sticks to boots is another practical check. Ruts with depths of 5 cm or more indicate that trafficability thresholds have been exceeded (Müller et al. 2011). In fact, historically “the earliest soil penetrometers were fists,18 thumbs, fingernails, pointed sticks, and metal rods,” used qualitatively (Davidson, 1965). These methods are extremely easy and free, no tools needed, instantaneous feedback. They directly en gage the notion of Atterberg limits (a practiced person can roughly tell if the soil is wetter or drier than the plastic limit by its feel). Limitations of hand tests include their subjectivity, as assessments depend on the operator’s expe rience, what one person describes as “firm” may be judged as “soft” by another. These tests also probe only the near surface and provide no quantitative measure of soil strength, offering impres sions rather than values with units. In addition, they cannot detect small gradations, essentially distinguishing only between plastic and friable conditions, which may be insufficient when the question is how many kilopascals of pressure the soil can withstand. Nonetheless, as a first ap proximation, if the soil is clearly plastic and sticky, traditional wisdom advises against traffic be cause it is likely to be weak. Digital Cone Penetrometers – The penetrologger: For a more quantitative assessment of soil strength, portable digital cone penetrometers such as the penetrologger (Royal Eijkelkamp, 2022) provide an intermediate solution between full laboratory testing and manual inspection. The pen etrologger measures the soil’s resistance to vertical penetration using a cone-tipped rod inserted at a constant speed. It records cone index (CI) values in megapascals (MPa), which reflect soil com paction and soil strength. These measurements can help determine whether a field is trafficable for machinery or too weak to support loads. The penetrologger system includes GPS integration for spatial referencing and can optionally rec ord soil moisture content via a thetaprobe sensor. The device is ergonomically designed for field use, supports depths of up to 80 cm, and allows for storage and graphical analysis of up to 1500 readings using accompanying penetroviewer software. Operators are required to maintain a steady19 insertion speed, typically 2 cm/s to ensure data reliability. A built-in speed indicator helps prevent excessive variability, which would otherwise compromise the data. Despiteits technical advantages, the penetrologger has known limitations. It oftenmeasures higher penetration resistance than what is actually experienced by roots, as roots can exploit macropores and grow around soil particles, while the cone cannot (Locher & De Bakker, 1990; Royal Eijkelkamp, 2022). Soilmoisture also plays a significant role in penetration resistance; wetter soils show lower resistance, which necessitates that measurements for comparison be taken under sim ilarmoisture conditions, ideally at field capacity(Royal Eijkelkamp, 2022). Additionally,thepres ence of stones or localized compaction may distort readings, reinforcing the need for multiple replicates per plot (Campbell & O’Sullivan, 1991; Royal Eijkelkamp, 2022). Lastly, although the penetrologger is effective for comparing treatments or management practices, variability in soil texture, structure, and organic matter content across different sites limits its use for absolute cross site threshold determination (Royal Eijkelkamp, 2022). 1.7 Management strategies to improve trafficability Effective management strategies are key to improving soil trafficability, ensuring that agricultural operations can proceed smoothly even under challenging conditions (Antille et al., 2016). Among the most commonly employed solutions are drainage systems (Evans & Skaggs, 1989), access mats (Thompson et al., 2022), and cover crops (Lal et al., 1991). 1.7.1 Drainage systems 1.7.1.1 Historical significance of drainage in Manitoba’s wet prairie Manitoba’s southern prairie was historically a vast wetland-dominated landscape that hindered early agriculture. By some estimates, about 60% of the Red River Valley lowlands were wetlands20 before settlement (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). Early settlers found much of southern Manitoba “maddeningly” waterlogged, with marshes and bogs making travel and farming difficult (Mani toba Agriculture, 2021). Wetlands were viewed as wastelands or, as historian Shannon Stunden Bower put it, “submerged farmland waiting to be drained” (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). To con vert this wet prairie into productive farmland, the provincial and federal governments undertook aggressive drainage initiatives in the late 19th century. AkeymilestonewasManitoba’sfirstDrainageAct of1880,whichempoweredtheyoungprovince to drain nine major wetland complexes (including the 47,000-acre St. Andrews Bog near Winni peg) (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). This monumental effort and subsequent projects rapidly elim inated most large marshes (e.g. today only fragments like Oak Hammock Marsh remain of St. Andrews Bog) and revealed the fertile black soils beneath (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). The 1885 Swamp Lands Act further targetedthedrainageorreclamationof anadditional 7 –10million acres of wet land across Manitoba (Manitoba Agriculture, 2010), a colossal area considering only ~17.6 million acres are farmed in Manitoba today (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). These policies trans formed Manitoba’s wet prairies into arable land, enabling settlers to farm areas that had been avoided due to excessive water. Contemporary accounts note that no one wanted to settle in south ern Manitoba until drainage made it habitable; indeed, many early immigrants bypassed the soggy RedRiverplain fordrierlandsfurtherwest (ManitobaAgriculture,2021).Thelarge-scaledrainage campaigns of the 1880s–1890s shaped the province’s development, sparking a boom in agricul tural settlement around the turn of the 20th century (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). In short, drain age was historically pivotal in converting Manitoba’s wet prairie into the productive agricultural landscape (particularly the Red River Valley) we know today.21 1.7.1.2 Surface vs. subsurface (Tile) drainage in Manitoba Agricultural drainage in Manitoba takes two main forms, surface drainage and subsurface (tile) drainage, each with distinct methods and applications. Surface drainage refers to managing water on the field surface via land shaping or ditches to quickly move excess water off fields. Its purpose is to prevent ponding and crop damage after snowmelt or heavy rain, routing runoff in a controlled manner to avoid erosion (Ma et al., 2024). Subsurface drainage involves installing buried perfo rated pipes (tile lines) below the soil surface to draw excess groundwater from the root zone. The tiles lower the water table across the field, which improves trafficability and crop growth on wa terlogged soils (Ma et al., 2024). Surface Drainage: In practice, surface drainage in Manitoba includes field drains, ditches, and leveling that remove standing water before it infiltrates. This approach is widely used and histori cally common. For example, over half (53%) of Manitoba field crop producers reported using some form of surface drainage by 2021 (Manitoba Government, 2023). Surface draining is espe cially useful on the heavy clay soils of the Red River Valley, where water tends to remain on the surfacedueto slow infiltration (ManitobaCropAlliance,2025). Keybenefits includereduced crop losses from flooding and earlier field access in wet seasons. However, surface drains cannot lower a high water table and thus may not dry the subsoil. They also require regular maintenance (clean ing ditches, etc.) and, on sloped terrain (> 0.5% grade), can increase erosion risk if not managed carefully (Manitoba Crop Alliance, 2025). Subsurface (Tile) Drainage: Subsurface drainage involves the installation of underground perfo ratedpipesdesignedto removeexcess gravitational waterfromthesoil,therebyloweringthe water table and improving field conditions (Manitoba Crop Alliance, 2025). This practice leads to drier root zones, warmer soils in spring, which can improve seeding conditions and crop yields. It also22 helps mitigate issues such as soil salinity by reducing prolonged saturation, which otherwise facil itates salt accumulation at the surface (Manitoba Crop Agriculture, 2016). Although historically underutilized in Manitoba due to its flat topography, high installation costs, andtheneedforspecializedoutletsorpumpingincertainareas,subsurfacedrainageisnowgaining popularity among producers who recognize its agronomic benefits (Manitoba Crop Alliance, 2025). Farmers report that tiled fields dry and warm up faster, which supports earlier planting and reduces yield losses in wet years. Research confirms the operational advantages of using subsurface drainage. For example, it has been associated with reduced soil water content and increased soil penetration resistance (1,197 kPa) compared to non-drained conditions (1,084 kPa), as shown in a Midwestern U.S. study (Kaur et al., 2023). Mante et al. (2018) further demonstrated that tile-drained fields had, on average, an 8.26% lower water table than undrained fields, with reduced volumetric water content at both 0.4 m (0.32 vs. 0.37) and 0.2 m depths (0.25 vs. 0.30). It should however, be noted that subsurface drainage requires careful planning and site-specific adaptation. Its effectiveness can be compromised by factors such as poor soil structure, improper installation depth,incorrect slope,orunsuitabledrain spacing (Cordeiro& Sri Ranjan,2015;Ayars et al., 2006). For example, drain spacing that is too wide may lead to under-drainage, while overly narrow spacing can cause over-drainageand soil waterstress (Cordeiro& Sri Ranjan,2015). Drain spacing wider than 15 meters led to increased delays during both spring and the growing season in a sandy loam soil, whereas spacings of 12 meters or less consistently resulted in fewer non workable days (Mante et al., 2018).23 Moreover, Manitoba’s flat landscape poses unique challenges in establishing adequate drainage gradients and outlets. Compliance with provincial regulations under the Water Rights Act adds another layer of complexity, as drainage projects often require permits and coordination with au thorities (Manitoba Crop Alliance, 2025). 1.7.2 Access mats Access mats (AMs), typically measuring 2.4 m × 4.2 m and weighing around 700 kg, are con structed from three layers of interlocking wooden boards, commonly spruce (Thompson et al., 2022). Engineered to withstandloadsexceeding thoseofthevehicles usedinfield operations, AMs are designed to protect soil surfaces and support trafficability by bearing the weight of heavy ma chinery (Thompson et al., 2022). Their use has been shown to mitigate soil compaction and help preserve soil water and nutrient levels. For example, research conducted at the Mattheis Research Ranch in Albertaassessed theeffects ofAMsonsoil andvegetation in theDryMixedgrass Prairie. Thestudyfoundthatwhileaccessmatseffectively reducedcompactioncaused byindustrialtraffic, prolonged deployment (beyond 12 weeks) led to a decline in grass biomass and an increase in ruderal species, opportunistic weeds that colonize disturbed soils (Thompson et al., 2022). This is likely due to increased nutrient availability under the mats, which favors the growth of such spe cies. Access mats are generally available for purchase or rental, with a range of products suited to dif ferent industries and terrain types. Costs vary based on size, material, and type, with wooden mats typically starting at around $7.62 per square foot. More specialized mats, such as rig or crane mats, command higher prices (terrapro, 2024). Practical limitations include logistics and cost (mats are heavy and require equipment for deployment, retrieval, transport, and storage), so their use in broad-acre cropping is usually confined to short travel corridors, field entrances, or localized wet24 spots. They can also trap moisture and reduce aeration/soil warming beneath panels, and care is needed to avoid moving soil and weed seeds between fields. Soil-engaging operations (e.g., pri mary tillage, strip-till, or opener passes) cannot be performed while mats are in place; mats must be removed before tillage, adding time and cost (Thompson et al., 2022; terrapro, 2024). 1.7.3 Cover crops 1.7.3.1 Historical adoption of cover crops Cover cropping is not a new practice. Use of cover crops dates back over several millennia, possi bly to the origins of agriculture (Lipman, 1912). Historical records indicate that cover crops were used as green manure by civilizations in Eastern Asia and ancient Rome approximately 3000 years ago (Groff,2015; Lipman, 1912). Ancientcivilizations utilizedleguminous covercropsto enhance soil fertility and productivity (Groff, 2015). Traditionally, cover crops were incorporated into the soil to accelerate decomposition and improve nutrient availability, making them synonymous with green manure (Groff, 2015). The advent of synthetic nitrogen fertilizers, facilitated by technological advancements during World War I, temporarily reduced the reliance on cover crops (Groff, 2015). Increased access to fertilizers and herbicides led to a decline in their use within conventional cropping rotations. How ever, growing concerns about soil carbon losses, soil degradation, nutrient runoff, and nitrate leaching from agricultural lands contributed to a renewed interest in cover crops in the 1980s (Dabney et al., 2001). Today, cover crops are gaining popularity among both organic and conven tional producers for their ability to build soil health, enhance organic matter content, and improve nutrient cycling (Morrison and Lawley, 2020).25 1.7.3.2 Cover crop use in Manitoba The growing interest in cover crops across Manitoba highlights their role in promoting sustainable agriculture by enhancing soil health, controlling erosion, managing nutrients, and suppressing weeds (Morrison and Lawley, 2020). In Ontario, a 2020 survey found that 520 farms planted 107,900 acres of cover crops, with 91% of respondents reporting agronomic or environmental benefits within three years of adoption (Morrison & Lawley, 2021). Across Ontario, estimates suggest overall adoption rates ranging from approximately 18.6% up to nearly 42% in some south western regions, averaging around 33.5% of farmers employing cover crops in 2016 (Schmalz & Robertson, 2020). By contrast, the adoption of cover crops faces challenges, particularly in regions like Manitoba, where short growing seasons, extreme temperature fluctuations, and unpredictable fall precipita tion create unique agronomic constraints (Manitoba Agriculture & Resource Development, 2021). These local limitations align with insights from recent global meta-analyses, which emphasize that cover crop outcomes are highly region-specific—yield and SOC benefits are greatest in humid– tropical systems, whereas in temperate or semi-arid regions, such as the Canadian Prairies, com petition for soil water can reduce yields (He et al., 2025). This reinforces the need for strategies tailored to Manitoba’s growing conditions. Reflecting these constraints, the 2021 Census of Agri culturereported that 20,330 Canadian farmsplantedwinter cover crops,but only 773ofthese were in Manitoba (Statistics Canada, 2021). Additionally, the University of Manitoba’s voluntary cover cropsurvey foundthat in 2020, 281 farmsacrossManitoba,Saskatchewan,andAlbertagrewcover crops on 41,496 hectares (Morrison and Lawley, 2020). While early adopters in Manitoba have integrated cover crops into their systems, questions about their viability and long-term benefits26 persist, reinforcing the need for ongoing research to address uncertainties and provide tailored recommendations. Beyond agronomic considerations, cost remains a key factor that influences cover crop adoption. The expenses associated with implementing cover crops in Manitoba vary depending on factors such as seed type, seeding method, and management practices. According to the 2020 Prairie Cover Crop Survey, many farmers reported spending between $5 and $30 per acre on cover crop seeds (Morrison and Lawley, 2020). For example, seeding red clover at a rate of 7 to 10 pounds per acre, with seed costs ranging from $2.50 to $3.50 per pound, results in an average seed cost of $17 to $35 per acre (Manitoba Government, 2024). In addition to seed costs, producers must consider fixed costs like land and machinery. Manitoba Agriculture’s 2025 Crop Cost of Production budgets report fixed land costs of approximately $103.67 per acre, with machinery fixed costs around $86.59 per acre across field crops (Manitoba Agriculture, 2025). These figures provide a region-specific basis for economic assessment. Moreover, land cost context is essential. Cover crops in Manitoba are typically planted in the shoulder season after harvesting and do not compete directly with cash crops for land use. They aregenerally integrated into existing crop rotations rather than plantedonnewlyacquiredorrented land. Consistent with findings elsewhere, farmers are less likely to establish cover crops on rented land, indicating that land competition is not the primary economic barrier to adoption (Bergtold et al., 2017; CTIC, 2023). Consequently, the main economic considerations for Manitoba producers lie in managing seed, machinery, and opportunity costs—such as the foregone value of fallow grazing or other land uses.27 1.7.3.3 Roles of cover crops Cover crops are plants grown primarily to protect the soil or improve soil conditions rather than for direct harvest, and they play a crucial role in improving soil health and water dynamics (Lal et al., 1991). Commonly used fast-growing annuals include cereal grains such as oats (Avena sativa L.) and fall rye (Secale cereale L.), along with brassicas such as forage radish (Raphanus sativus L. var. longipinnatus) and legumes including field peas (Pisum sativum L.) and clovers (Trifolium spp.) (Clark, 2007). In Manitoba, fall rye, oats, and field peas are considered reliable choices for fall cover cropping (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). Many growers also use cover crops in a dual-purpose manner for forage. For example, approxi mately 46%ofPrairiecovercropusersterminated theircovercropsbygrazinglivestock(Morrison & Lawley, 2020), demonstrating their widespread use as annual forage. When grown solely for cover rather than harvested, cover crops represent an added input cost (seed, planting, termination) without direct revenue, typically on the order of tens of dollars per acre, which remains a key barrier to adoption (Snapp et al., 2005; Morrison & Lawley, 2020). One of the key benefits of cover crops is their ability to influence soil water dynamics, which may significantly improve soil trafficability by reducing excess soil water (Kaspar et al., 2001). Through their root systems and transpiration, cover crops can lower soil water content. For in stance, Mupangwa et al. (2021) found that in the semi-arid climate of the U.S. Southern Great Plains, soil water content was 22–26% lower in cover crop plots compared to no-cover plots at termination. This reduction in water is particularly valuable in spring, when field operations de pend on improved trafficability for planting and harvest. The reduction in soil water is achieved throughtranspiration (Kasparet al.,2001)andthroughthecreationofsoilbiopores,whichenhance water infiltration and percolation (Pan et al., 2018).28 1.7.3.4 Effects of cover crops on soil water use The effectiveness of cover crops in reducing soil water is influenced by factors such as root density (Blanco-Canqui & Ruis, 2020), leaf area index (LAI) (Chen & Black, 1992), and relative humidity (Kucharik et al., 2000). A higher root density allows cover crops to extend their root systems over alargersoilarea,improvingwaterextractionandincreasingmacroporosity,whichfacilitatesbetter infiltration and water movement (Blanco-Canqui & Ruis, 2020). Additionally, a higher LAI in creases the surface area for transpiration, further contributing to water reduction (Chen & Black, 1992). However, the capacity of cover crops to reduce soil water also depends on climatic condi tions and the specific type of cover crop used (He et al., 2025). For example, cover crops with higher biomass tend to have greater evapotranspiration rates, leading to increased soil water de pletion. A study in South Carolina found that a five species mixture and rye produced the highest biomass at the end of the season, ranging from 4,600 to 6,480 kg ha⁻¹ (April 19, 2017, in the first season and May 10, 2018, in the second season), and exhibited the highest water use efficiency (WUE) values between 2,184 and 2,232 g m⁻³. In contrast, lower biomass cover crop such as crimson clover and a crimson clover-turnip mixture, generated significantly less biomass (1,344 to 2,271 kg ha⁻¹) and had much lower WUE values (207 to 685 g m⁻³) suggesting a reduced ca pacity to extract soil water (St. Aime et al., 2020). In regions with limited water, cover cropping can result in water deficits, as observed in a Texas sorghumfarmstudywhereinsufficientrainfallbetweentheplantingandterminationofcovercrops led to significant soil water depletion and cash crop failure (Kasper et al., 2022). Despite these challenges, cover crops remain a valuable tool for improving soil water management and prevent ing soil compaction, particularly in wet conditions (He et al., 2025). Their use as green mulch can also help conserve water by slowing down evaporation, providing additional benefits in water-29 scarceregions(Steenwerth&Belina,2008).However,carefulmanagementisnecessarytobalance water reduction with the potential risks of soil water stress and crop yield loss in dry conditions. Further research on cover crop practices, especially in semi-arid regions is needed to better under stand their impact on water use and overall soil health. 1.7.3.5 Types of cover crops 1.7.3.5.1 Grass cover crops Grass cover crops, such as cereal rye , barley (Hordeum vulgare), oat wheat (Triticum aestivum), millet (Panicum miliaceum), and triticale (× Triticosecale), are widely recognized for their fibrous root systems, which enhance soil structure and prevent erosion (McGourty & Reganold, 2005; Blanco‐Canqui & Jasa, 2019). These grasses establish rapidly, providing ground cover that mini mizes soil disturbance and limits weed competition (Blackshaw, 2008). Additionally, they act as nitrogen scavengers, reducing nitrate leaching and improving nutrient cycling efficiency (Ruffo & Bollero, 2003; Blanco-Canqui et al., 2015). In temperate climates, fall rye has demonstrated superior biomass production in the spring com pared to other cereals, with fall-seeded rye producing significantly higher residue before cash crop planting (Blackshaw, 2008). Similarly, oat and fall rye cover crops have shown effective nitrate uptake after corn and soybean harvest, reducing soil NO₃⁻ concentrations by 30–60 kg N ha⁻¹ in the top 0–60 cm soil profile compared to no-cover controls (Blackshaw, 2008). This reduction is agronomically beneficial because it decreases the risk of nitrogen losses through leaching and de nitrification, thereby conserving nitrogen for subsequent crops. Environmentally, lower residual nitrate minimizes the risk of groundwater contamination and eutrophication of surface waters. Winter wheat and triticale can also survive, but only if seeded early by mid-late August, and they generally produce less biomass and survive fewer extreme cold events of -15o C (Martens & Entz,30 2011; Blackshaw 2008). Notably, rye residue appears less inhibitory to dry bean germination than wheat or triticale (Flood & Entz, 2009). This is important because dry beans are a high-value crop in Manitoba and are particularly sensitive to residue-mediated germination issues. Selecting cover crops such as rye, which pose less risk of inhibiting dry bean emergence, is therefore critical for farmers integrating cover crops into rotations that include pulses. This compatibility suggests that rye can be incorporated into rotations without compromising bean yields, in contrast to wheat or triticale residues, which may interfere with seedling establishment. Moreover, the ability of rye to suppress weed germination while allowing dry beans to establish successfully makes it an attrac tive option for sustainable weed management in bean production systems (Flood & Entz, 2009). Hairy vetch (Vicia villosa), a nitrogen-fixing legume, offers spring N benefits but has inconsistent winter survival. North Dakota trials reported survival as low as 10% in some years (Kucek et al., 2024). This poor survival was attributed to extreme environmental conditions, notably minimum temperatures falling below −22 °C combined with the accumulation of more than 110 freezing degree days (FDD) in the absence of snow cover (Kucek et al., 2024). Rye often produces 1.8 – 2.1 times more biomass than vetch as found on a fine loamy soil in Beltsville, Maryland when terminated in late May (Poffenbarger et al., 2015). Brassicas like forage radish (Raphanus sativus L. var. longipinnatus) and winter rapeseed (Brassica napus L.) provide bio-tillage and nutrient scavenging, but most winterkill in Manitoba (Elmy, 2020). Winter camelina (Camelina sativa) shows promise for overwintering (Wittenberg, 2020), though still less reliably than rye. In trials conducted across the Upper Midwestern U.S., winter camelina exhibited survival rates ranging from 72% to 79%. The species has a cold-tolerance of −29°C (Chao et al., 2023). Mixtures such as rye–vetch are commonly used to combine the complementary benefits of cereal and legume species, including improved nitrogen fixation, weed suppression, and soil cover31 (Clark, 2007). For instance, Creamer et al. (1997) reported biomass yields ranging from 0.8 to 3.6 t ha⁻¹ for rye–vetch mixtures seeded in late August in Columbus, Ohio. However, translating such outcomes to Manitoba requires caution. Local success is strongly influenced by seeding date, with early establishment being critical for adequate biomass accumulation and overwinter survival. Studies by Hayden et al. (2015) and Odhiambo & Bomke (2001) emphasize that late planting significantly reduces establishment success and winter hardiness in cold-climate regions such as Manitoba, underscoring the importance of timely seeding for mixture performance. 1.7.3.5.1.1 Fall rye As noted in section 1.7.3.4.1, fall rye (Secale cereale) is one of the few winter crops that can over winter in temperate climates. As such, it can be used to manage soil water in regions with chal lenging excess soil conditions (Krueger et al., 2011). As an overwintering cover crop, fall rye is capable of thriving in light sandy soils, where it contributes to stabilizing erosion-prone areas through its extensive root system and slow-decomposing straw (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). While sandy soils are among the environments where fall rye performs well, they are not necessarily its optimal growing conditions (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). Fall rye pro duces best on fertile, well-drained soils of medium texture (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016).Nonetheless,itremainshighlyadaptableandcontinuestoyieldreliablyunderlessfavorable conditions, including soils with high acidity, low fertility, and both heavy and light textures often outperforming other commonly grown cereals in such environments (Alberta Agriculture and For estry, 2016). Its ability to survive fall and winter seasons, even under low temperature conditions, ensures continuous soil protection when no cash crops are present (Basche et al., 2016). By utiliz ing latesummer,fall, and earlyspring waterthatwould otherwisegounderutilizedbyspringcrops,32 fall rye helps reduce soil water levels and creates more favorable conditions for planting and other fieldwork (Basche et al., 2016). To establish properly before winter, fall rye should be seeded shallow, preferably at 1 to 1.5 inches (2.5 to 3.8 cm) deep into a firm, moist seedbed (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). Fall rye sown at a 1-inch depth has twice the emergence rate of that sown at 2 inches, with shallow-seeded rye exhibiting greater winter hardiness (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). However, when seeding shallowly, residue from the previous crop can interfere with seed-to-soil contact, making even residue distribution of the preceding crop essential for quick emergence (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). In Manitoba, the recommended seeding date for fall rye is between August 25 and September 25 to ensure sufficient growth prior to winter and the onset of vernalization (Manitoba Agriculture & Resource Development, 2021). The provincial seeding rate recommen dations range between 50 to 100 kg ha⁻¹ at a seeding depth of 2.5 to 5 cm (Manitoba Agriculture & Resource Development, 2021). Seedingtimingiscritical foroptimizingfallryeperformance(Blackshaw,2008).Seedingtoo early can lead to excessive vegetative growth, reducing grain yield and lowering the 1000-kernel weight (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). Conversely, seeding too late can result in lower yields, delayed heading, reduced plant height, and poor winter survival (Evans et al. 2016). The climate plays a crucial role in fall rye’s development, requiring at least four to six consecutive weeks of daytime temperatures above 10°C for adequate growth before cold hardening (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). During this period, fall rye accumulates approximately 309 fall growing de gree days to reach the anthesis stage, with top growth typically reaching 3 to 4 inches in height. These developmental milestones are essential for successful overwintering and vigorous spring growth.33 Fall rye’s winter hardiness and early maturation (Clark, 2007) allow it to overcome late summer droughts and frosts, further enhancing its role in water management (Qi & Helmers, 2010). Addi tionally, its competitive nature against weeds reduces the need for herbicides, promoting healthier soil conditions (De Bruin et al., 2005). Fall rye remains an invaluable tool for reducing soil water, particularly in the spring when improv ing trafficability is essential for efficient field operations. Its ability to thrive in adverse conditions, protect soil, and manage water makes it a critical component of sustainable farming practices, especially in regions with variable climates and soil types. 1.7.3.5.1.1.1 Spring termination of fall rye: Methods and impacts Before planting spring crops, fall rye must be terminated to avoid competition. The primary meth ods are chemical termination, mechanical termination, and strip tillage (Oliveira et al. 2019). Each method affects how much biomass remains, how soil and water conditions are altered, and ulti mately how the following crop performs. Termination timing (early kill vs. planting green) is crit ical and often dictated by spring rainfall and soil trafficability (Oliveira et al. 2019). Chemical termination, usually using glyphosate, is the most common approach. In Nebraska, 96% of farmers used glyphosate to terminate rye ahead of soybean (Oliveira et al. 2019). Glyphosate is effective within 1 – 2 weeks when applied under suitable weather (Ruffo et al., 2004). It preserves all rye biomass as surface mulch, supporting soil health and weed suppression. De Bruin et al. (2005)foundno yield differencein soybean regardlessofherbicidestrategy andRuffoet al. (2004) reported soybean yields (~2.8 Mg/ha) were unaffected by termination timing. However, non-leg ume crops may suffer from nitrogen immobilization or soil water depletion if rye is terminated too late. Thus, early termination (~10 – 14 days pre-planting) is advised for cereals, while soybeans can be planted green with minimal yield impact (Stephens et al., 2023).34 For mechanical termination, mowing or roller-crimping is popular in organic systems. Mowing is effective at flowering but may allow regrowth if done earlier (Evans et al., 2016). Evans et al., (2016) used flail mowing post-planting in dry beans. While weed suppression was excellent, bean yields declined due to nitrogen depletion and delayed growth compared to a winterkilled oat con trol. Roller-crimping requires rye to reach anthesis, which often coincides with or follows Mani toba’s optimal planting window. Although it avoids herbicides and preserves surface residue, me chanical termination often requires multiple passes or careful timing to avoid regrowth and soil cooling challenges, particularly in wet springs (Evans et al., 2016). Strip tillage offers a hybrid solution by creating tilled seed zones while preserving rye between rows. This method allows faster soil drying and warming while maintaining residue benefits. In Janzen’s (2023) study, strip tilling mitigated soybean yield loss when rye was terminated late. In their study, they observed that yields reached ~2.7 Mg/ha with strip till versus < 2.0 Mg/ha without it. For dry beans, strip till helped only modestly. Because only the row zone is cleared, strip till leaves partial cover, which can balance water conservation and improve trafficability. While it requires specialized equipment, strip till enables greater flexibility and improved outcomes under variable spring conditions. 1.7.3.5.1.1.2 Interaction between fall rye and Manitoba’s climate Manitoba’s Prairie climate is challenging for overwintering cover crops, yet fall rye is compara tively well suited because of its exceptional cold tolerance and ability to resume growth early in spring. Its performance, however,depends on conditions across phases—fall establishment,winter survival, and spring regrowth. Key climatic drivers include the amount and persistence of snow cover (insulation), the frequency and intensity of freeze–thaw cycles, exposure to temperature ex tremes relative to rye’s hardiness, and the timing and distribution of precipitation.35 Snow cover is a critical moderator of winter soil temperature. A fall rye cover crop can signifi cantly affect snow distribution (Clark, 2007). Its tall, upright stubble traps snow across the field (Clark, 2007). This snow layer insulates the soil and the rye plants, buffering them against extreme air temperatures. In fact, rye’s rapid late-season growth aids snow capture, which boosts its winter hardiness (Clark, 2007). Trapped snow keeps soil temperatures more moderate and can lead to a shallower frost penetration. Under a cover crop, the frozen soil layer by spring can be shallower than in bare soil (e.g. 0.4 m vs 0.6 m) (Kahimba et al., 2008). By maintaining an insulating snow blanket, fall rye increases its likelihood of surviving Manitoba’s winter. Notably, winter rye is often chosen over winter wheat in the windiest Prairie areas because it tolerates open, snow-free conditions that would kill less hardy cereals (Clark, 2007). This robustness under variable snow cover makes rye dependable in Manitoba’s climate. Repeated freeze–thaw cycles are common in late fall and spring. Fall rye withstands these cycles due to physiological cold hardening. It begins cold acclimation at relatively mild temperatures in the fall, enabling it to survive when temperatures plummet (Werle et al., 2017). Rye is documented as the most winter-hardy cereal grain, tolerating temperatures as low as –34°C once well estab lished (Grubinger, 2021). Consequently, rye plants typically endure Manitoba’s deep freezes and can resume growth when conditions warm. The cover crop can also influence the soil’s freeze– thaw behavior. By late winter, soils under a rye cover have been observed to be slightly warmer (by 0.1 – 0.4 °C to 0.8 m depth) than bare soils, promoting earlier thaw in the topsoil (Kahimba et al., 2008). An earlier spring thaw means the soil is receptive to infiltrating water when snowmelt occurs, rather than remaining ice-sealed. However, freeze–thaw cycles can have a downside for nutrient dynamics. Ryeand othercovercropsaccumulatebiomass that,uponfreezing andthawing, starts to decompose. This process can release nutrients like phosphorus from the plant tissue. In36 Manitoba, the risk of dissolved phosphorus loss in spring runoff may increase if rye residue decays during freeze–thaw events (PAMI, 2022). This potential trade-off (reduced erosion P loss but in creased dissolved P in snowmelt water) is an active area of study. Agronomically, the freeze–thaw resilience of fall rye ensures consistent ground cover and soil protection through winter, but man agement must consider the timing of nutrient release as the climate shifts to spring. Fall rye’s growth cycle is adapted to Manitoba’s temperature regime. It can germinate and grow at temperatures just above freezing (Grubinger, 2021), allowing establishment in late fall. Once dormant in winter, rye’s crowns tolerate extreme cold. By spring, the rye breaks dormancy early. Manitoba often experiences fluctuating spring temperatures, warm spells followed by hard frosts. Rye is relatively unfazed by late frosts; its growing points remain near the soil early on, and it is accustomed to cold snaps (comparable to spring wheat tolerating –8 to –10 °C at the seedling stage).Thus, rye reliably provides early-springbiomass. Its winterhardiness andearlyspringvigor mean that even in short growing seasons, it can accumulate significant biomass by mid-spring when other cover species would just be restarting (Clark, 2007). This contributes to benefits like weed suppression and erosion control, which are tied to the cover’s biomass production (Clark, 2007). Nevertheless, temperature and water availability in the fall determine how much rye grows beforewinter.Manitoba’sfallscanbeshort;insomeyearsanearlyfreezetruncatesfallryegrowth. Such limitations mean the rye enters winter with suboptimal biomass or development, potentially reducing its spring vigour (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). This highlights the influence of fall temperature patterns on rye performance, a warm, long autumn will permit stronger estab lishment than an unusually cold or early winter onset. The timing and amount of precipitation (fall and spring) have pronounced effects on fall rye. In the fall, adequate soil water is needed for germination and early growth. One common challenge37 is fall drought. 27% of Prairie farmers in a survey cited lack of fall water as a problem for estab lishing cover crops (Morrison & Lawley, 2020). If September to October are too dry, rye may germinate poorly or grow sparsely, diminishing its overwinter survival and benefits. On the other hand, excessive rainfall in fall can delay rye seeding (if cash crop harvest is late on wet fields) or waterlog young seedlings. Rye is fairly tolerant of moist soils, but saturated conditions combined with cold can increase risk of diseases or ice damage (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016). In spring, precipitation effects are double-edged. A wet spring (heavy snowmelt or rain) can lead to waterloggedtopsoils that delayseeding ofmaincrops. Insuch cases, aliving fall ryecoveractively growing can be advantageous, it continues to transpire water and dry the soil in April–May, accel erating the readiness of the soil (Clark 2007). By using up excess soil water, rye can improve trafficability and create better seedbeds for planting. Indeed, agronomists recommend allowing a rye cover to grow longer in a wet spring to “help dry the soil” before termination (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). However, in a dry spring, that same water use by rye can become detrimental. If spring rainfall is below normal, an actively growing rye cover may deplete soil water needed by the subsequent crop. The balance is delicate. For example, Carman field experi ments documented that where spring was dry, even an early-terminated rye cover left soil drier and led to yield penalties in the following crop (Flood & Entz, 2018). Conversely, under wetter than-average spring conditions, the rye cover improved yield by mitigating excess water (Flood & Entz, 2018). These findings highlight how precipitation interacts with rye’s water use. As a result, management guidelines in the region emphasize flexibility: terminate the rye cover 14 days before planting in normal or dry conditions to conserve water, but consider a later termination if fields are overly wet (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). Fall rye’s quick spring maturity also means it can be killed in time to avoid drawing water from late-spring rains needed for the main38 crop (Clark, 2007). Overall, fall rye’s agronomic performance (biomass production, persistence, and impact on the next crop) is tightly linked to Manitoba’s climatic swings. Successful use of this cover crop requires understanding these climate interactions, snow cover patterns, freeze–thaw cycles, and precipitation variability, and adjusting management (seeding date, termination timing) to align with the conditions of a given year. 1.7.3.5.1.1.3 Integrating fall rye into annual cropping systems: Feasibility and subsequent crop impacts Incorporating fall rye as an overwinter cover crop in Manitoba’s annual cropping rotations is ag ronomically feasible but requires careful management. While fall rye’s hardiness and flexible planting window make it a popular cover in northern regions (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Grow ers, 2021), the Prairie climate and crop schedule impose limitations. Farmers must weigh the ben efits of rye (erosion control, weed suppression, soil improvement) against potential drawbacks for the following cash crop. Key considerations include establishment timing and rotation fit, spring resource use, and allelopathic effects and planting interval. Successful integration of fall rye hinges on finding a planting window after the main crop harvest. Rye can be sown from late summer into October in cold regions (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers,2021),allowinguseafterearly-harvestedcrops(e.g.cereals,forage,silagecorn)orshort season pulses. However, if the preceding cash crop is harvested very late (as with grain corn or sunflower), there may be insufficient time for rye to establish before winter. In practice, many Prairie farmers report the short growing season and dry fall as major hurdles for cover crop estab lishment (30% cite a short fall window; 27% cite lack of fall water) (Morrison & Lawley, 2020). This suggests that while fall rye is capable of fitting into annual systems, its realistic adoption39 depends on yearly conditions and crop sequencing. Sowing rye immediately after harvest and un der adequate water is critical for it to become well established and survive winter. Producers often target fields where the preceding crop is harvested early, typically by August or early September, and theymay “startsmall”to gain experiencewith thetiming(ManitobaPulseand Soybean Grow ers, 2021). Overall, fall rye can be integrated into annual rotations on the Prairies, but the timing of cash crop harvest and fall weather will determine how reliably it can be incorporated each year. A living rye cover in spring can significantly affect soil resources for the next crop, which can be an advantage or disadvantage depending on conditions. Soil water is a prime example. Fall rye’s rapid spring growth uses substantial water, which helps dry out waterlogged soils but can exacer bate drought in a dry spring (Elmy, 2020). In wetter-than-average springs, allowing rye to grow longer is actually beneficial – the cover crop acts as a water “sponge” that can improve trafficabil ity and creates a better seedbed for planting into wet clay loams (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). However, if spring rainfall is below normal, that same water uptake can leave the soil too dry for the emerging crop. Flood & Entz (2018) observed substantial yield penalty where dry bean seed yield fell by 46%, from 2659 kg ha⁻¹ in a no rye treatment to 1428 kg ha⁻¹ where rye was present. Thus, farmers must adjust termination timing based on water, terminating rye about 14 days before seeding is recommended in normal or dry springs to conserve water, whereas in a wet spring a later kill can be used to strip excess water (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). Nitrogen (N) immobilization is another concern. A high-biomass cereal cover like rye ties up nitrogen as it decomposes, potentially depriving the next crop of early N. The later the rye is terminated(and themore matureits biomass),thegreatertheN tie-up; it is noted thatincorporating a large rye cover can immobilize significant N (Clark, 2007). This is especially problematic for N demanding follow-up crops (e.g. corn, wheat, canola) that need readily available N at seeding40 (Manitoba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). To avoid this, agronomists advise ending the rye cover by early spring growth stages (e.g. by stem elongation) so that its biomass remains more succulent and N-rich (Clark, 2007). Killing rye earlier limits its spring biomass, thereby reducing both water use and nitrogen lock-up. In situations where a substantial rye cover is grown, farmers may need to compensate with higher starter N fertilizer or companion legumes. Planting rye in a mixture with a legume (like vetch or clover) is one strategy used elsewhere to offset rye’s N im mobilization, the legume can contribute nitrogen and mitigate yield loss, allowing the cover to be left growing a bit longer (Clark, 2007). Rye is known for its allelopathic properties. It releases biochemical inhibitors that suppress the germination and growth ofotherplants. This is part ofhow rye covercropshelp with weedcontrol, but it can also inhibit small-seeded cash crops following in the rotation if proper caution is not taken. For instance, rye residue has been documented to reduce germination of sensitive crops like alfalfa and canola (Clark, 2007). To prevent allelopathic injury to the subsequent crop, farmers are advised to allow a gap between terminating rye and planting the next crop. A conventional guide line is to kill the rye cover about 10 – 14 days before seeding the cash crop (Clark, 2007). This waiting period gives time for phytotoxins in rye residues to break down and for the soil to warm and dry sufficiently, ensuring better conditions for seedling emergence (Manitoba Pulse and Soy bean Growers, 2021). In practice, the “two-week rule” has become a standard in Manitoba when rye is followed by yield-sensitive or small-seeded crops (e.g. canola, corn, spring wheat) (Mani toba Pulse and Soybean Growers, 2021). In one Saskatchewan trial, even a modest rye cover caused a 15% reduction in canola plant stand, likely due to a combination of residue effects and water competition (Saskatchewan Ministry of Agriculture, 2022). By terminating earlier and de laying planting, producers minimize the risk of rye’s allelochemicals or heavy mulch hindering41 the new crop’s germination. It’s worth noting that if the next crop is a legume like soybean or pea, there is more flexibility, these larger-seeded, N-fixing crops are less sensitive to rye’s N tie-up and allelopathic residues. In Manitoba, studies found that soybean yields were unchanged whether rye was terminated 14 days before planting or theday ofplanting (ManitobaPulseand Soybean Grow ers, 2021). Closely monitoring soil conditions and seedbed quality is crucial if shorter intervals are used, as rye’s thick residue can interfere with seeding equipment and soil warming if not managed. 1.7.3.5.1.1.4 Rye’s role in supporting water infiltration and percolation Fall rye cover crops can significantly influence soil water movement, particularly infiltration (the entry of water into soil) and percolation (downward movement through the soil profile). In the context of Manitoba and similar Prairie soils (often heavy clay loams prone to runoff), fall rye can provide several hydrological benefits (Kahimba et al., 2008). Its impact on infiltration and perco lation is mediated through improvements in soil physical properties and active water uptake (Kahimba et al., 2008). Key mechanisms includeroot-inducedmacroporosity, improvedsoil struc ture and organic matter, and surface residue effects on runoff and snowmelt. Fall rye develops a deep and fibrous root system, even in cold conditions. Its roots can penetrate compacted soil layers more effectively than many cash crops (Ren et al., 2019). As these roots grow and later decompose, they leave behind continuous channels (macropores) that act as con duits for water. These macropores facilitate the rapid infiltration of water into deeper horizons, bypassing the dense surface layer that typically impedes water entry in clay loam soils. In long term no-till systems, the cumulative effect of cover crop roots and earthworm channels greatly increases the soil’s macroporosity (Myers et al., 2019). Studies elsewhere have quantified substan tial increases in macropore volume (on the order of 1 – 67% more macropores) due to cover crop ping (Çerçioğlu et al., 2025). For Manitoba’s poorly draining soils, even a modest increase in42 macropore connectivity can mean the difference between water quickly moving down the soil profile versus ponding on the surface. Deeper root penetration by rye also helps break up dense subsoil,providingpathwaysforpercolationintodeepersoillayerswherewatercansafelybestored or slowly released (Kahimba et al., 2008). In essence, fall rye’s roots act as natural “biological augers,” loosening tight soils and creating a network of pores that supports enhanced infiltration. The presence of a rye cover crop contributes organic matter to the soil, both through root exudates and decaying residues, which in turn improves soil structure. Greater organic matter leads to better aggregation of soil particles. Strong, stable aggregates increase the proportion of larger pores and reduce soil bulk density, thereby improving the soil’s capacity to absorb water (Çerçioğlu et al., 2025). A critical review of cover crop impacts found that integrating covers into annual rotations can reduce soil bulk density by up to 24% and markedly improve soil structural indices (Çerçioğlu et al., 2025). Over several seasons, fall rye can help build a more friable topsoil with reduced surface crusting. This matters for infiltration because tightly packed or crusted topsoil will repel or shed water, whereas an open, crumbly structure lets water in (McPhee et al., 2015). Addition ally, higher soil organic carbon from rye residues can enhance soil water retention at depth (by increasing microporosity), while still promoting rapid infiltration at the surface (via macroporos ity). In the long term, as soil organic matter increases and tillage is minimized, the soil develops better aggregate stability (Steenwerth & Belina 2008). Field measurements have indeed shown improved aggregate stability and hydraulic conductivity with continuous cover crop use (Hao et al., 2023; Myers et al., 2019). Notably, improved structure also confers greater soil strength, a well-aggregated soil can bear equipment loads without compacting as easily (Myers et al., 2019). This feedback loop means that soils under cover crops are less likely to reconsolidate or seal, preserving their infiltration-friendly structure even under the weight of spring field operations. In43 Manitoba’s clay loams, which are naturally prone to compaction and surface sealing, the structural enhancement from rye is especially valuable for maintaining infiltration pathways. A fall rye cover (and its residue) protects the soil surface and modulates how water from precipi tation or snowmelt enters the soil. The living cover and post-winter residue intercept raindrops, reducing their energy and preventing soil crust formation. Instead of pounding the soil into a seal, rain is gentler when a mulch or cover is present, allowing more water to soak in (McPhee et al., 2015). Residues from rye also slow down overland flow. During spring snowmelt, rye stubble can significantly influence hydrology (Kahimba et al., 2008). It tends to distribute snowmelt water more evenly and slows its movement, granting extra time for infiltration. Moreover, rye covers can lead to soil thawing earlier in spring (Kahimba et al., 2008). This timing is crucial: when the snow melts, if the soil is already thawed (thanks to the cover crop’s effects), the water can infiltrate instead of running off a frozen surface (Kane et al., 1991). In a study at Carman, researchers ob served that plots with a fall cover crop absorbed snowmelt and had higher deep percolation, whereas adjacent bareplots (fallow)hadmorerunoffandretainedwaternear thesurface(Kahimba et al., 2008). Another analysis noted that increased soil water content under a cover was attributa ble to residues trapping more snow, which subsequently melted and infiltrated (Kahimba et al., 2008). Essentially, the rye cover acts like a sponge and a buffer during snowmelt events. It catches and holds snow, then facilitates its entry into the soil profile (Kahimba et al., 2008). This can mitigate the flash runoff and flooding often seen in Prairie spring thaws. However, it is worth noting that if a rye cover is very dense or the soil beneath remains partially frozen, there could be cases of perched water (Mendis et al., 2022). For example, if rye establishment was poor and root channels wereinsufficient, watermight accumulate aboveastill-frozenor compact subsoil (“water44 bathtub” effect) (Kane et al., 1991). Thus, the positive impact on snowmelt infiltration is maxim izedwhenthecovercropiswell-establishedandsoilprofilethawissynchronizedwithmelttiming. Empirical evidence supports these qualitative effects. Field studies in comparable continental cli mates have found that introducing cover crops can increase infiltration and reduce runoff within a few years (Koudahe et al., 2022). For example, a study in the U.S. Midwest reported that just 2 – 3 years of cover cropping led to > 50% higher infiltration rates on silty clay loam soils (Patton et al., 2024). By promoting infiltration and percolation into the soil profile, rye not only reduces immediate flood and erosion risks but also contributes to recharging soil water at depth in preserv ing water for dry periods (Patton et al., 2024). This dual role of mitigating excess water at the surface while improving subsoil water distribution makes fall rye an important tool for managing water on Prairie soils. 1.8 Research opportunities for leveraging overwintering cover crops in Manitoba Overwintering cover crops, especially fall rye (Secale cereale L.), hold promise for improving soil and water management in Manitoba’s cold climate. The specific objectives were to i) evaluate the impact of fall rye on off-season soil water and heat dynamics, ii) assess the impact of cover cropping on soil strength to support spring field traf ficability, and iii) determine the potential for fall-rye establishment in Manitoba.49 1.10 Thesis outline This thesis was prepared in a manuscript format following the thesis guidelines of the Department of Soil Science, University of Manitoba. 2 EVALUATING THE IMPACT OF FALL RYE ON SOIL STRENGTH TO SUPPORT SPRING FIELD TRAFFICABILITY IN MANITOBA 2.1 Abstract Excess soil water during Manitoba’s early growing season, driven by snowmelt and heavy rainfall, poses significant challenges for achieving the soil strength necessary for trafficability and timely field operations. Subsurface drainage is a widely used strategy to remove excess soil water; how ever, its effectiveness is often limited by poor internal drainage and compromised soil structure. Fall shoulder cover crops, such as fall rye, have been proposed as an alternative approach to regu late soil water and enhance soil resilience against extreme weather conditions. A multi-year field studywasinitiatedin2020nearCartwright,MB,toassesstheeffectivenessoffallryeinimproving soil conditions for trafficability. From 2022 to 2024, fall rye has been grown as a fall shoulder cover crop, and continuous soil water measurements were collected from fall seeding through spring termination at five depths (0 – 5, 5 – 15, 15 – 25, 25 – 35, and 45 – 55 cm). The critical soil water content for supporting trafficability was defined as 90% of the lower plastic limit within the 0 – 25 cm layer. Additionally, historical climate data (2015 – 2023) were analyzed for a September 21, the earliest seeding at the site, to evaluate the success of fall rye establishment based on key temperature thresholds, requiring at least four consecutive weeks with daily average temperatures above 10°C and the accumulation of 309 fall growing degree days to reach anthesis. The results demonstrated that the utilizing fall rye cover crop did not improve the soil condition based on the soil water criterion necessary for soil trafficability, primarily due to suboptimal fall growing con ditions limiting establishment. HYDRUS model simulations further showed that while delayed termination of rye until June 20, chosen to coincide with the Manitoba Agricultural Services Cor poration Excess Moisture Insurance seeding deadline resulted in limited windows, up to 21 days,72 where soil moisture met the trafficability threshold, these periods were vulnerable to rewetting from subsequent rainfall. These findings highlight the limitations of fall rye as a standalone strat egy for managing excess soil water in Manitoba. To optimize the benefits of cover crops in cold climates, an integratedapproach that leverages adaptiveagronomicpractices andconsidersclimate variability is necessary to enhance soil trafficability and soil water management. 2.2 Introduction Spring field operations in Manitoba are frequently constrained by excess soil moisture, which lim its trafficability and delays seeding of cash crops. This issue is typical of soils in this region during the early growing season due to snowmelt and heavy rainfall, with the region receiving on average 69.97 cm of snowfall during the off-season and 102.55 mm of rainfall in early spring (Weather Spark, 2025; Manitoba Agriculture, 2025). Approximately 47% of soils in Manitoba, primarily those classified as Classes 3 to 5, have poor internal drainage characteristics (Eilers et al., 2002), exacerbating the risk of excess water accumulation. The resulting delays in spring operations can be substantial. For example, in years when spring rainfall exceeded evapotranspiration by 37% or more, between 6 and 52 non-workable field days were recorded, significantly affecting trafficabil ity and the timeliness of field activities (Mante et al., 2018). Managing soil water to attain optimal soil strength for trafficability is therefore crucial to ensure timely field operations in the region. Delayed field operations reduce the limited growing season due to limited frost-free days in Man itoba, typically between 75 and 135 days (Manitoba Agriculture & Resource Development, 2021). Such delays can result in crop yield losses because farmers are unable to seed within the optimal planting window. In 2022, for example, excess soil water restricted field access, leading to an 8% reduction in intended seeded acreageduringthecropproductionseason (ManitobaMarkets, 2022). These impacts extend beyond reduced yields to delays in harvesting. In 2016, due to excess soil73 water, harvesting was suspended in majority of the southern grain belt of the Prairie Provinces (Alberta Agriculture and Forestry, 2016; Saskatchewan Government, 2017). Ultimately, such con sequences could jeopardize farmers' livelihoods. Spring cereals planted after mid-May lost ap proximately 1% of yield per day of delay, which, at average yields and prices, translates into an estimated CAD 4–5 per acre per day in lost revenue. Similarly, canola yields may decline from about 95% to 80% of potential when planting is postponed into mid-June (Manitoba Agricultural Services Corporation, 2022; Barker, 2008; Juskiw & Helm, 2002). With an average size of 1,177 acres per farm in Manitoba (Statistics Canada, 2021)), a loss of CAD 4,708 and 5,885 could be incurred per day by a farmer due to poor soil trafficability. Thesoil’scapacity to providetraction andsupport forheavymachinery (trafficability) (Earl,1997) has become even more important considering the increasing trend of machinery weight over the years. Such trend has led to increased risk of soil deformation (Schjønning et al., 2015). For in stance, the typical weight of a combine harvester rose from approximately 4,000 kg in the 1950s to over 35,800 kg by 2020, contributing to significantly higher ground pressure and an increased risk of soil compaction (Keller & Or, 2022). Soil deformation, including rut formation, compac tion, and puddling damages topsoil and reduces soil porosity, hinders water infiltration and perco lation, and increases runoff. Compaction also limits root development and soil aeration which may enhance greenhouse gas emissions. Domzal & Hodara (1992) observed a decrease in total porosity by 14 and 5.5 v/v % within the0-10 cm and10 - 20 cm layers, respectively duringafield operation (one pass by a tractor), indicating a significant decrease in air filled-porosity. Usually, areas with poor trafficability are the most affected during extreme weather conditions, as was the case in Saskatchewan in 2006. That year, excessive water from snowmelt and heavy rainfall rendered74 approximately 1.8 million acres too wet to seed, leading to crop insurance payouts exceeding $60 million (Western Producer, 2006). The occurrence and extent of poor soil trafficability depend on soil strength, mainly influenced by soil water. Wet soils reduce soil strength, thus limiting their ability to support traffic (Hamza & Anderson, 2005). Over the years, subsurface drainage has been popular for managing excess soil water in Manitoba. However, its feasibility is limited by poor internal drainage and compromised soil structure (Kokulan, 2019). Against this setback, farmers in Manitoba have long sought strate gies to mitigate the potential adverse effects of spring field traffic on soil health and productivity (Manitoba Crop Alliance, 2025). Among these strategies, cover cropping has emerged as a promising practice. There is significant interest and adoption of cover cropping practices among farmers in the region based on a 2020 Prairie cover crop survey (Morrison & Lawley, 2020). The survey demonstrated that 37% of the 281 early adopters who participated in the survey grow cover crops in Manitoba. Farmers integrate cover crops by planting in late summer or early fall to protect the soil over winter—maintaining residue that reduces wind erosion and traps snow, reduces crusting, and preserves aggregate sta bility—thereby supporting spring resilience and structural integrity (Haruna et al., 2020). While the benefits of cover cropping on soil health and water management are well-documented, its specific impact on soil strength and soil trafficability in Manitoba remains unexplored. Also, as promising as cover cropping may be, questions linger about its effectiveness under Manitoba's unique climate and agronomic practices, where the semi-arid conditions, short photothermal win dow, high spring relative humidity, non-uniformly distributed precipitation often lead to poor fall establishment—lowbiomassandinadequategroundcover—whichunderminesitsbenefitsforsus tainable production.75 Hence, the objective of this research was to evaluate the effectiveness of overwintering cover crop (fall rye) in improving soil strength to support soil trafficability in Manitoba. This study is critical to unravelling strategies to improve soil resilience and agricultural productivity in the region. 2.3 Materials and methods 2.3.1 Soil evaluation and modelling for the field experiment at Cartwright 2.3.1.1 Study site To evaluate the effects of overwintering cover cropping on soil strength, with a focus on its impli cations for spring field trafficability in Manitoba, an experimental study was designed to compare the impact of fall rye on off-season soil water and heat dynamics at Cartwright, Manitoba. 2.3.1.1.1 Cartwright The experimental study was conducted at a farm site in Cartwright (Figure 2.1), located near the Rural Municipality of Roblin in south central Manitoba at the location, NW-28-2-14 W1 as indi cated in Figure 2.2 with GPS coordinates N49°09.582 W099°17.998. The elevation of the study area is 464 m above sea level (a.s.l) (Google Earth, 2024). The soils at the study area are classified as theDarlingford series, composed of well-drained orthicblack chernozem originatingfrom mod erately to strongly calcareous, deep, uniform, loamy (L, CL, SiCL), mixed till deposits (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). The textural class of the soil is clay loam with an average organic matter con tent of 5.9% within the 0 – 25 cm layer. In a standard Darlingford soil profile, the solum is approx imately 60 cm thick with the parent material typically being yellowish-brown mixed till featuring a limited number of coarse fragments (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). The site is situated in middle to upper positions on very gentle slopes within undulating to rolling landscapes (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013) with a slope of 2 - 5%. Such landscape can result in a76 higher cultivation costs compared to a flat landscape and also increase the risk of water erosion (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). These soils have medium to moderately slow permeability, which causes slow water transmission. This can lead to waterlogging, making trafficability challenging (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013) and potentially delaying seeding and harvesting dates at the study site. The site is equipped with a tile drainage system (Figure 2.3), installed in 2013, to manage excess water across the field. However, some areas over the south ridge were not tiled due to their landscape position. Tile drains are installed at varying depths across the field, reflecting the undulating topography. In low-lying depressions, the tiles are positioned at depths of approximately 1.2 to 1.5 meters (4 to 5 feet), while on higher ground or ridges, installation depths reach up to 3.7 meters (12 feet) to maintain appropriate slope and drainage function throughout the landscape. Given the clay loam texture of the soil and its inherently low permeability, a tile spacing of 15 meters (50 feet), even at shallower depths such as 0.9 meters (3 feet), is likely inadequate for rapidly removing gravitational water to field capacity, particularly following significant rainfall or snowmelt events (Mante et al., 2018). The slow lateral movement of water in these fine-textured soils limits drainage efficiency; and evapotranspiration alone is unlikely to remove excess water quickly enough to allow for timely spring field operations (Wankmüller et al., 2024). At an elevation of 464 m, the study area has relatively moderate temperatures with a 30-year cli mate normal of 1.3°C (Environment Canada, 2024). According to the climate normals for Cart wright, the average high temperatures for April, May, June, July, and August over the past ten years have been 16°C, 22°C, 25°C, and 25°C, respectively, while the average low temperatures for the same months were 6°C, 12°C, 15°C, and 14°C (Weather World, 2024). These conditions77 support the cultivation of cereals, legumes, and brassicas during the summer, which are rotated with cover crops in the fall (Bourgault et al., 2022). The site has been seeded with different crops over the years: soybeans in 2022, canola in 2023, and wheat in 2024. In fall 2023, fall rye (open pollinated) was seeded at a rate of 118 kg/ha, whereas in fall 2022 it was seeded at a lower rate of 39 kg/ha. The fall rye was allowed to overwinter and was terminated with glyphosate herbicides usingagroundsprayerbeforeplantingcash cropsforthenextgrowingseason. Therewas notillage at the site. The fall rye seed was drilled to a seeding depth at 1.9 cm. Rainfall in the region totalled 495 mm in 2022, then dropped significantly to 200 mm in 2023, and increasing to 484 mm in 2024 with the 30-year climate normal being 366 mm (Environment Can ada, 2024). The average air temperature was 1.9°C in 2022, rose to 3.6°C in 2023, and was 2.8°C in 2024, with all three years exceeding the 30-year climate normal of 1.3°C (Environment Canada, 2024). Additionally, over the past two years (2022/23 and 2023/24), the average soil temperature at the top 5 cm from fall to spring was -0.4°C. These soils occur in mid to upper slope positions of undulating to hummocky landscapes, where surface runoff is moderate to moderately rapid and permeability is moderately slow (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). In a typical Newdale soil profile, the solum is characterized by a very dark gray Ah horizon that is commonly about 25 cm thick (ranging from 15 to 35 cm), a dark brown Bm horizon 10 to 30 cm thick, and a transitional BC horizon 3 to 15 cm thick (Manitoba Food and Rural Initiatives, 2008). In shallower profiles, a lime carbonate horizon 10 to 15 cm thick is sometimes present, although it is absent in deeper profiles (Manitoba Food and Rural Initiatives, 2008). Overall, the solum depth averages approximately 58 cm, ranging from 25 to 90 cm (Manitoba Food and Ru ral Initiatives, 2008). Minor components of well drained Eluviated Black Chernozem soils, marked by thicker combined A horizons (30 to 60 cm) and Bt horizons up to 40 cm thick are also included within the Newdale mapping units (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). The study area is set on gently undulating terrain typical of the Inglis region. Native vegetation historically consisted of intermixed aspen groves and grassland, a reflection of the conditions un-100 der which the Newdale soils formed (Manitoba Agriculture, 2013). Today, these soils are pre dominantly cultivated, supporting a variety of crops suited to the moderately well drained condi tions and the distinct solum development (Manitoba Food and Rural Initiatives, 2008). The gentle slopes and hummocky topography not only influence drainage patterns and surface runoff but also necessitate tailored field management practices such as the use of tile drainage systems in areas prone to localized waterlogging. Visually, the model captured the changes in soil water content over time at all depths during both the calibration and validation periods. The simulated water patterns generally followed the ob served trends, demonstrating the model’s capability to reflect water fluctuations under field con ditions. This is further supported by the statistical evaluation of model performance, which in cluded the Nash–Sutcliffe efficiency (NSE), coefficient of determination (R²), RMSE–observation standard deviation ratio (RSR), and the percent bias (PBIAS). The Nash–Sutcliffe efficiency (NSE) is a dimensionless statistic used to evaluate how well the simulated data match observed measurements by comparing the residual variance to the variance of the observed data (Nash and Sutcliffe, 1970). NSE values range from negative infinity to 1 where values closer to 1 indicate more accurate and reliable model predictions. In this study, NSE values were 0.94 during calibration and 0.95 during validation both of which fall well within the acceptable range. Values above 0.5 are generally considered satisfactory (Gupta et al., 1999). The coefficient of determination (R²) evaluates how well the simulated values align with measured data by assessing the degree of collinearity with values ranging from 0 to 1 (Gupta et al., 1999).126 Figure 2.19 Visual comparison of observed and simulated soil water content127 Higher R² values indicate a stronger correlation and better model performance. In this study, R² values were 0.56 for calibration and 0.52 for validation across all depths both exceeding the 0.5 threshold generally considered indicative of acceptable model performance (Moriasi et al., 2007). Similarly, the slope and y-intercept of the best-fit line were close to 1.0 and 0.0, respectively, indicating a strong match between the observed and simulated soil water content as shown in Fig ure 2.20. Figure 2.20 Correlation between observed and simulated soil water content The RMSE–observation standard deviation ratio (RSR) is the ratio of the root mean square error (RMSE) to the standard deviation of the observed data and hence assesses model performance by quantifying the residual error. In this study, RSR values were 0.23 for calibration and 0.24 for validation indicating very good model performance as shown in Table 2.6. RSR ranges from 0 to positive infinity, where a value of 0 indicates no residual error and represents perfect model per formance (Moriasi et al., 2015). As RSR increases, model accuracy decreases. Generally, RSR128 values below 0.5 are considered acceptable, with lower values reflecting reduced RMSE and im proved simulation accuracy (Moriasi et al., 2007). As presented in Table 2.6, PBIAS values were generally within the acceptable ±10% threshold, with validation falling well within the range (4.9%), while calibration showed a slightly higher deviation at 14.3%, indicating minimal bias and very good simulation accuracy. Positive PBIAS valuessuggest aslightunderestimationofsoilwatercontentbythemodel,whereasnegativevalues would indicate overestimation (Gupta et al. 1999). Lower PBIAS values are desirable, as they reflect smaller deviations and greater accuracy (Moriasi et al., 2007). Despite the commencement of snowmelt event, the frost-free period, which spans between the last spring frost and the first fall frost is a critical determinant of agricultural planning and crop success. The frost-free period de fines the window during which crops can grow without exposure to damaging frost, thereby influ encing planting and harvesting schedules, crop variety selection, and long-term management strat egies (National Climate Assessment, 2014). In the context of cover cropping, understanding the frost-free window is essential to ensure fall rye achieves sufficient fall establishment for cold hard ening and regrows adequately in spring before termination. The last day of frost is defined as the final day when the minimum air temperature is equal to or below 0°C. In Year 1 of this study, the frost-free period began on May 4, 2023 and April 1, 2024 in year 2. According to climate normals, there is a 90% probability that the last spring frost in this region occurs on or after May 13 highlighting the potential for lingering frost events well into mid-May. This persistent frost risk can significantly constrain the early spring development of fall rye. In Year 1, fall rye was seeded on October 4, and in Year 2 on September 21— both dates falling outside the optimal seeding window recom mended for maximizing fall growth. As a result, the crop failed to accumulate sufficient growing degree days (GDD) before the onset of cold temperatures, inhibiting its ability to reach the devel opmental stages necessary for overwintering success. Fall rye performance in both years was char acterized by consistently low biomass accumulation of 100 kg/ha, limited root development, and an average leaf area index (LAI) of 0.47. These values contrast markedly with other Manitoba based studies (Flood &Entz, 2018; Evans et al., 2016), where timely seeding resulted in biomass yields exceeding 1000 kg ha⁻¹, sufficient for both cover and function. The discrepancy highlights the importance of planting date as a primary driver of successful establishment. A biomass thresh old of 1000 kg ha⁻¹ is commonly cited as the minimum required to achieve approximately 30% ground cover- an essential benchmark for delivering effective soil conservation services (Larney et al., 1997). Similarly, an LAI of 3 is generally required to ensure complete canopy closure and support maximum evapotranspiration (ET) under non-limiting soil water conditions. For soil ero sion control specifically, a minimum LAI of 0.9 has been recommended (Howell et al., 1997). The observed LAI of 0.47 in this study fell well below both the conservation and evapotranspiration thresholds confirming that the late-seeded fall rye did not develop a sufficiently dense canopy to provide meaningful protective or hydrological benefits. As there was a limited vegetative devel opment offall rye, theobserved cropevapotranspiration(ETc)remainedlow throughout thespring period with maximum values not exceeding 1.3 mm day⁻¹. These rates are insufficient to support substantial removal of water from the soil profile before field operations.135 In addition, infiltration capacity measured in the cover crop (CC) plots (10.8 cm/hr) did not sig nificantly differ from that of the no-cover crop (NCC) plots. This lack of differences suggests that the rye root system did not reach a level of development capable of enhancing soil porosity or facilitating macropore formation- two key mechanisms through which overwintering cover crops are hypothesized to influence water infiltration and percolation (Myers et al., 2019). In this study, soil mechanical resistance was assessed to a depth of 55 cm with particular focus on the 0 – 25cm layer where a critical threshold of 3MPa is commonly referenced as the limit beyond which seedling emergence and early root growth may be hindered (Müller et al., 1990; Leenaars et al., 2018). The measured penetration resistance values never approached this 3 MPa threshold (Table 2.5), which would typically suggest that soil mechanical impedance was not a limiting factor for seedling emergence or root penetration in spring. It is important to note, however, that these measurements were taken under wet soil conditions, during which penetration resistance tendsto besignificantly reduced dueto thelubricating effect of water. Assuch,therecordedvalues may not reflect the actual soil strength that seedlings or roots would encounter under drier condi tions. Therefore, resistance values were below the critical 3 MPa limit at the time of measurement; they may underestimate mechanical constraints that could emerge as the soil transitions to lower water states. Taken together, these findings indicate that under the prevailing climatic conditions at Cartwright characterized by late seeding, insufficient fall GDD, and restricted crop development, fall rye was unable to deliver meaningful conservation benefits or hydrological improvements. These limita tions reinforce the importance of appropriate seeding timing and favorable fall weather in enabling fall rye to function effectively as a conservation and soil management tool during spring field operations.136 2.4.6.6 Extent and consistency of fall rye establishment across Manitoba Long-term weather variability impact on fall rye establishment was assessed with seeding date on September21atCartwright,Inglis,andSteinbach.Thecriteriaforsuccessfulfallryeestablishment were not met in any year from 2015 to 2023 across Cartwright where the study site is located (Figure 2.23). The study combined field measurements from a two-year experiment with long-term climate analysis and HYDRUS 2D model simulation. The results showed that while soil water dynamics were in fluenced by soil depth and date, there was no significant impact of the fall rye (cover crop) treat ment on soil water content in either year. Soil water content averaged 44.6% in year 1 and 37.5% in year 2 across both cover crop and no-cover crop treatments. These values exceeded 90% of the lower plastic limit of the soil (24.3%), the threshold for sufficient soil strength to support traffica bility. Consequently, the presence of cover crops did not improve soil trafficability. This lack of effect can be attributed to the low biomass production of the fall rye, which was insufficient to enhance evapotranspiration and/or infiltration rates. Forsoilheatdynamics, thepresenceofcovercropsinfluencedheat fluxinyear1,withasignificant crop by date interaction observed. On Day 17, soil heat flux in the no-cover crop treatment was 49% higher than in the cover crop treatment, likely due to the insulating effect of the cover crop. However, in year 2, no significant crop effects were detected on soil heat flux, which averaged 0.18 MJ/m²/d across both treatments. With no significant impact of cover crops on soil water and heat dynamics, the resultant effects on water dynamics were minimal. The anticipated benefits of faster soil warming and reduced heat transfer due to the presence of fall rye were not realized, and evapotranspiration rates remained low, with maximum daily ETc values not exceeding 1.3 mm day⁻¹ in spring. Long-term climate analysis (2015 – 2023) with seeding date of September 21 revealed that envi ronmental conditions in Cartwright and other regions of Manitoba (Northwest and Eastern zones)143 consistently failed to support successful fall rye establishment. Neither the necessary four consec utive weeks with mean daily temperatures ≥10°C nor the required 309 GDD before cold hardening were met. As a result, fall rye generally failed to accumulate sufficient biomass in fall, which compromised its potential for influencing spring soil conditions. HYDRUS 2D model simulations showed that when fall rye termination was delayed to June 20, isolated windows of up to 21 days emerged where soil moisture in the upper 0 – 25-cm layer met trafficability criteria. However, these windows were highly susceptible to rewetting from subse quent rainfall events, limiting their reliability for field operations. The findings suggest that while fall rye did not significantly impact soil water or heat dynamics, the depth and date effects were notable, highlighting the importance of soil profile and seasonal environmental conditions. The low biomass production of the cover crop was a limiting factor in its effectiveness. Further research is needed to explore the influence of higher biomass-producing cover crops and alternative management practices to enhance soil conditions for early-season traf ficability. 2.4.9 References Allen, R. 3 OVERALL SYNTHESIS 3.1 Summary of findings and implications of research Excess soil water during Manitoba's early growing season, primarily caused by snowmelt and heavy rainfall, creates significant challenges for achieving adequate soil strength to support traffi cability and timely agricultural operations. Poor trafficability delays fieldwork, disrupts crop seed ing, and shortens the already limited growing season, which spans only 75 to 135 frost-free days (Manitoba Agriculture, 2021). These disruptions can lead to substantial crop yield losses, as seen during the 2022 production season, where an 8% reduction in intended seed acreage was recorded (Manitoba Markets, 2022). Delayed planting also delays harvest, amplifying the economic and operational strain on farmers and threatening their livelihoods. To address these challenges, this study explored the potential of fall shoulder cover crops, partic ularly fall rye, to improve soil resilience and support trafficability under excess water conditions. The multi-year research examined the effectiveness of fall rye in regulating soil water and enhanc ing soil strength. However, findings revealed that the cover crop treatment did not sufficiently reduce soil water or improve soil strength to support spring field trafficability under the studied conditions. Historical climate data (2015 – 2023) highlighted critical limitations in fall rye establishment due to Manitoba’s variable climatic conditions. A September 21 seeding date frequently failed to meet the four-week requirement of daily average temperatures above 10°C or accumulate the necessary 309 fall growing degree days for adequate biomass growth before cold hardening. These subopti mal conditions often hindered fall rye establishment, limiting its effectiveness as a standalone so lution for managing excess soil water.154 The findings highlight the need for a more integrated and adaptive approach to managing soil trafficability in Manitoba. While fall rye offers potential benefits for other agronomic purposes, such as providing surface cover and preserving the soil pores, its utility in supporting spring field trafficability under Manitoba's climatic conditions with September 21 seeding timeline is limited. Complementary strategies, such as adjusting cover crop seeding timing, adopting water manage ment practices like subsurface drainage systems to suit the soil profile, and exploring alternative cover crop species better suited to the region’s climate should be considered. Tools such as soil water meters and the promotion of plastic limit testing in commercial laboratories could also em power farmers to make data-driven decisions about soil conditions and trafficability. This research highlights the complex interaction of climate, soil, and management practices in enhancing soil trafficability. It emphasizes that effective solutions require tailoring strategies to regional conditions and integrating social, agronomic, economic, and environmental systems. Pol icymakers, agronomists, and farmers must carefully assess the risks and benefits of implementing fall rye-based strategies while considering their tolerance for variability in outcomes. In response to the broader challenge of mitigating climate change, one of the most significant threats to global food security, this study contributes valuable insights toward achieving Canada’s commitment to net-zero emissions by 2050 (Government of Canada, 2024). By advancing knowledge on sustainable land management practices and informing cover cropping strategies, this research provides a foundation for optimizing agricultural practices in cold-climate regions. Future research and management practices can build on these findings to enhance soil trafficabil ity, reduce operational delays, and support sustainable agriculture in Manitoba and beyond.155 3.2 Recommendation Based on the findings from this research, it is recommended that: 1. Further research should be conducted to optimize cover crop seeding dates and termi nation methods to maximize their benefits for soil management. The timing of seeding and termination of fall rye was critical in this study as late seeding and insufficient cold hardening affected fall rye establishment and performance. It is critical to align the selection of cover crops species and seeding strategies to climatic conditions and agro nomic management to maximize the effects of cover cropping on improving soil strength for spring soil trafficability. 2. Integrated management strategies should be adapted to meet agronomic goals while leveraging the strengths of cover crops in soil conservation, soil resilience and traffica bility. Manitoba has variable spring weather conditions, which may lead to poor traffi cability due to re-wetting of the soil prior to seeding. Strategies such as implementing surface and subsurface drainage systems could complement the effects of cover crops in reducing soil water content in spring. 3. Agronomic strategies should leverage the benefits of cover crops throughout the year through intercropping and underseeding in addition to shoulder crops. This approach, however, should take into account the cash crop/ cover crop combination and the po tential risks of diseases and pest. Also, the impact of the cover cropping, termination methods and their impact on subsequent cash crop must be critically considered. 4. Given the dynamic nature of soil water and trafficability challenges in the study region, it is essential to evaluate the potential of living mulch as an alternative to conventional stubble management. Living mulch systems could provide continuous soil cover and156 enhance water regulation whereas stubbles trap moisture, which may present a signifi cant trafficability challenge in wet spring. 5. Since trafficability is a key concern for most fields in Manitoba for spring field opera tions, the environmental benefits, economic viability and practicality of implementing cover crops with and without other management strategies should be evaluated. 3.3 References Government ofCanada, 2024.
