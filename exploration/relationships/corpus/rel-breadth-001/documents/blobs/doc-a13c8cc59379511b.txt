As the global population is expected to reach 8.9 bil lion by 2050, agricultural systems, especially in developing countries, will face growing pressure to produce more food sustainably [170]. It is estimated that the global cereal equivalent (CE) food demand will increase from 10,094 million tons in 2030 to 14,886 million tons by 2050 [113]. Meanwhile, malnutrition remains a problem: in 2020, 149.2 million children under five were stunted, 45.4 million wasted, and 38.9 million over weight [301]. In response, sustainable agriculture focuses on maintaining soil health worldwide, acknowledging the vital role of soil microorganisms in preserving soil quality and productivity. Plant growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR), arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF), cyanobacteria, and beneficial nematodes are among the diverse organisms that support nutrient transformation, phytohormone production, plant resilience against abiotic stresses, and nutrient cycling within the soil system. Under the soil security framework, crop diversification, intercropping, and crop rotation are seen as effective strategies. However, achieving long-term adaptation requires updating agricultural production methods to current climate conditions, which calls for comprehensive, system-wide actions and significant policy and institutional changes at both national and local levels. According to the IPCC [111], climate change is expected to raise global tem peratures by 1.5 °C by 2050 and between 2 and 4 °C by 2100. This increase is likely to cause notable impacts, including temperature fluctuations, shifts in rainfall patterns, ris ing sea levels, freshwater shortages in coastal areas, and more frequent extreme weather events [71]. For example, climate-driven drought in eastern India has already begun to reduce regional crop yields. During the severe 2022 drought in Bihar, rice production dropped roughly 23% [196]. Additionally, in India, rising temperatures, altered rainfall patterns, and increasing CO2 levels are altering the incidence, severity, and geographic spread of major crop pests and diseases [284]. Water demand is projected to increase by 20–30% by 2050 [40], and economic losses due to floods (32%) and droughts (7%) have been significant between 1970 and 2023 (STATISTA, [274]). Climate impacts vary greatly by region and crop, with central India experiencing larger fluctuations in rice yields, which increases South Asia’s overall vulnerability [209, 242]. Meanwhile, exces sive use of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides has caused degradation of agroecosystems and water contamination, threatening aquatic life and human health [66]. In response, sustainable adaptation strategies are being developed globally [54]. Sustainable agri culture combines biological, chemical, physical, and ecological principles to maintain productivity while protecting environmental quality [2]. Although maximizing yields is essential, farming systems must equally deliver ecological, social, and economic advan tages. Low-yielding areas present opportunities for targeted productivity improvements, achievable through organic, conventional, or hybrid approaches [74]. In this context, scientists play a crucial role in translating soil health research into practical tools for sustainable land management. Sharing their expertise enables grow ers to adopt evidence-based practices. Regenerative Agriculture (RA) is one approach that enhances soil health, biodiversity, and watershed function while improving overall ecosystem services [280]. It also aims to reduce atmospheric carbon accumulation by increasing carbon sequestration in soils and plants, complementing other sustainable practices such as climate-smart agriculture and conservation agriculture [283]. The main philosophy of RA is to do more with less land, fewer chemical inputs, reduced water use, lower GHG emissions, and less degradation [162]. A key part of sustainable land management is assessing and protecting soil health, which includes the biological, chemical, and physical qualities that support productiv ity and vital ecosystem functions. It is affected by both natural and human-made fac tors, influencing agriculture, biodiversity, and recreational areas. Important indicators include microbial diversity, stability, activity, and abundance, with soil microbes, such as bacteria and fungi, playing vital roles in nutrient cycling and plant growth (Vander [3, 148, 167, 290]). Research over seven years has shown that organic farming increases soil microbial carbon compared to conventional methods [62], while practices such as tillage and moisture conservation also significantly impact soil health and structure [137, 245]. The mutually beneficial relation ships between soil microbes and plants, including mycorrhizae and PGPR, are crucial for improving nutrient uptake under stressful conditions [19, 151, 263–265]. Still, ongo ing cultivation and unsustainable land use continue to harm soil health [294], emphasiz ing the urgent need to restore or protect it, especially in the face of climate uncertainty. Exploring beneficial plant–microbe interactions and stress-tolerant microbial strains offers great potential for climate-resilient farming. Sustainable crop production must now consider both past and emerging climate variability [154]. Institutional support and effective strategies are essential for addressing climate risks and promoting agricultural sustainability. This review examines the role of beneficial microbes in improving soil health, identifies best practices in soil management, and proposes soil health indicators to expand climate-smart agriculture for sustainable development. 2 Methodology This systematic review followed the PRISMA framework to identify, screen, and synthe size evidence on soil health management strategies under changing climate conditions (Fig. 1). A comprehensive literature search was conducted across four major academic databases, Scopus, Web of Science, ScienceDirect, and Google Scholar, which yielded 2977 peer-reviewed records. An additional 1957 documents were retrieved from insti tutional and organizational repositories, including FAO, UNEP, and ICAR, to ensure the inclusion of grey literature and region-specific reports. The search strategy used com binations of keywords, including soil health, soil quality, soil carbon, regenerative agri culture, climate-smart agriculture, land degradation, soil microbial communities, and climate change adaptation. All retrieved records were imported into a reference man agement system, and duplicates were removed. After deduplication, 2007 records from databases and 1147 records from institutional sources remained for initial screening. Titles and abstracts were screened for relevance, scientific rigor, and thematic alignment, Fig. 1 Search criteria and methodological framework considered under the systematic literature review through a modified version of the PRISMA“preferred reporting items for systematic reviews and meta-analyses”guidelines and rules. Full texts of the remaining studies were assessed in two phases. In the first phase, 657 peer-reviewed articles and 457 institutional documents were evaluated for eligibil ity. After removing an additional 1147 duplicates, 1114 full-text records proceeded to detailed assessment. Studies were excluded if full texts were unavailable, published before 2000, or lacked quantitative or qualitative soil health indicators; this accounted for 125, 75, and 562 exclusions, respectively. Studies were included if they focused on soil health, biological, chemical, or physical soil indicators, climate-smart soil manage ment, or soil-based climate resilience; presented empirical, modelling, experimental, or meta-analysis data; and reported measurable soil outcomes related to carbon dynamics, nutrient cycling, structural improvements, or ecosystem services. Studies were excluded if they lacked empirical evidence, focused on non-agricultural soils, lacked relevant soil metrics, or fell outside the thematic scope. After applying all inclusion and exclusion cri teria, 914 studies were deemed suitable for qualitative assessment, and 352 high-quality studies were included in the final synthesis. These studies formed the evidence base for evaluating soil process dynamics, microbial functioning, carbon sequestration potential, and climate-resilient soil management practices. 3 Soil biodiversity and ecosystem sustainability Soil, though occupying only a small portion of Earth’s surface and containing a limited amount of organic matter, harbors an extraordinary diversity of life, particularly in its topsoil. Notably, more than 25% of all terrestrial life exists within the soil, highlighting its critical role in sustaining food production, mitigating pollution, supporting aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, enabling nutrient cycling and retention, and regulating the climate [199]. However, soil biodiversity faces escalating threats from land degradation, pollution, urbanization, mismanagement, and climate change—pressures that mirror those impacting broader natural resources. Among soil’s biological components, plant roots and microorganisms are key drivers of ecosystem functioning, with microbes accounting for 60–80% of soil activity, including nutrient cycling and decomposition of organic matter [44]. A sustainable soil ecosystem, essential for robust crop growth, relies on the complex interplay between soil microorganisms, structural properties, and plant systems. Gaining deeper insight into microbial communication and functionality is cru cial to prevent adverse outcomes associated with poor soil management [250, 275]. Due to its immense biological diversity, soil is considered one of the largest reservoirs of biodiversity on Earth [20]. This biodiversity plays a fundamental role in ecosystem sustainability—providing habitats, regulating climate, preserving water quality, control ling pollution, and enhancing food security [26, 220, 275]. Soil biota also boosts ecologi cal stability by influencing plant diversity, productivity, and species interactions, thereby improving ecosystem resilience [20, 275]. In particular, root-associated microorganisms help plants tolerate climate-induced stresses such as drought and temperature extremes. However, soil biodiversity is increasingly at risk in densely populated and intensively cul tivated regions, where biodiversity loss, overgrazing, chemical pollution, and land degra dation are major concerns [26]. Protecting soil biota is therefore essential for achieving sustainable agricultural goals and requires proactive conservation strategies [275]. Importantly, the value of soil biodiversity extend far beyond academic interest; it is increasingly utilize to address critical local, regional, and global issues, includingPage 5 of 39 Joshi et al. Discover Soil (2026) 3:39 biodiversity loss. Recognizing soil as a dynamic, living system encourages the develop ment of integrated, cross-sectoral solutions. Both microscopic and macroscopic soil organisms drive vital ecosystem processes by cycling nutrients and energy, thereby supporting plant and animal life across terrestrial ecosystems. Nature-based solu tions rooted in soil biodiversity, such as conserving natural landscapes, rehabilitating degraded land, promoting sustainable farming practices, and reimagining urban spaces for human and ecological benefits, provide powerful ways to address climate change, biodiversity loss, and sustainability [21]. As we aim for a sustainable future, managing this essential and diverse resource beneath our feet is crucial. 4 Soil health components for sustainable agriculture 4.1 Distribution of soil microorganisms Healthy soil sustains a diverse community of organisms, including vertebrates, earth worms, mites, insects, nematodes, fungi, bacteria, and actinomycetes [295]. These organisms interact closely, forming a complex web within the soil ecosystem. While soil fungi may exceed 100,000 species, only a fraction, ranging from 18,000 to 35,000, are formally described. Nematodes and mites represent about 3% and 5% of total species, respectively [35]. Estimating bacterial and archaeal species is challenging due to clas sification inconsistencies and cultivation difficulties [55]. Soil’s diverse organisms greatly enhance its biodiversity, interacting intricately with plants. Soils support a wide array of microorganisms and larger organisms, including plants and fauna, provided that a suf ficient carbon source is available to fuel their activity. Bacteria are plentiful but have a small biomass due to their size, while actinomycetes have comparable biomass but are fewer and larger. Fungi, though fewer in number, contribute significantly to overall soil biomass [259]. In tilled soils, bacteria, actinomycetes, and protozoa dominate due to greater tolerance to soil disturbance [75, 300]. Soil total biomass generally falls within the range of 8–15 t ha⁻¹, with variability driven by soil type, organic matter content, and land-use practices [116]. Nitrogen-fixing microorganisms, such as cyanobacteria and Rhizobium, play a vital role in enhancing soil fertility, as illustrated in Fig. 2 [136]. The rhizosphere around active roots houses over 30,000 bacterial species, indicating a posi tive link between organic compounds and microorganism population [289]. Discover Soil (2026) 3:39 especially in forest soils known as “living soil” due to their rich organism concentration [116]. 4.2 Farming practices to improve soil health Ensuring soil well-being is fundamental to achieving agricultural productivity and sus tainability. Soil health extends beyond crop yields to influence food safety, human and animal health, and water quality [224]. Effective management requires integrating phys ical, chemical, and biological indicators to assess the impact of farming practices and climate on soil functions, especially nutrient and carbon cycling. Even minor changes in soil organic carbon (SOC) per unit area can significantly affect the global carbon bal ance. Enhanced SOC stability improves soil structure, water and nutrient retention, buffering capacity, and microbial activity [159]. To support sustainable farming, the [289] outlines four core soil health principles: minimize disturbance, maximize soil cover, enhance biodiversity, and maintain living roots. These guidelines serve as a practical framework for improving soil function. Crop residue management, for example, can increase SOC, whereas certain cultivation prac tices may reduce SOC by accelerating the breakdown of physically protected carbon [23, 121]. Various conservation practices relevant to this are summarized in Table 1. 4.2.1 Maximizesoilcover Four essential farming techniques that play a key role in preserving soil cover and improving soil health are: (1) cover cropping, (2) mulching, (3) residue retention, and (4) green manuring. One such technique, cover cropping, involves growing crops with canopies that intercept rainfall and reduce soil erosion [127]. Cover crops, especially legumes, efficiently fix atmospheric nitrogen by partnering with rhizobium bacteria, making nitrogen available to plants. For example, cowpea can fix 25–30 kg ha− 1 yr− 1 of nitrogen [266]. Non-legumes, such as mustard, can serve as cover crops, too, inhibiting soil-borne pests with compounds like glucosinolates [108]. Mulching is another impor tant practice that reduces water loss, controls erosion, and maintains favourable soil tem perature and moisture conditions [130]. Residue retention, which involves leaving crop residues on the soil surface, protects the soil, supports microbial activity, and enhances soil moisture and carbon sequestration [152]. In general, the application of NPK and NPK + FYM results in increased biomass production and thus carbon input into the soil by 3–10 and 5–20 times, respectively, compared with no fertilizer addition [122]. Crop biomass production is negatively affected by poor nutrient availability under continuous cropping, thereby lowering productivity [262]. In a central Indian vertisol, Lakaria et al. [159] found that carbon addition to the soil increased from 228 to 332, 369 to 539, 44 to 65, and 256 to 374 kg ha− 1 yr− 1 through soybean leaf fall, roots, nodules, and rhizo deposition. When soybeans were treated with a 50% soil test crop response (STCR) dose plus a 5 t FYM treatment, the maximum C addition was observed. Similarly, the con tribution of carbon from wheat crop through stubbles, roots, and rhizo-deposition has been observed to be 1033, 131, and 1446 kg ha− 1 under the 50% STCR dose + 5 t FYM application. Finally, green manuring, which involves incorporating whole plants into the soil, improves organic matter content, enhances water retention, and stimulates micro bial activity [180]. Crop rotations diversify food sources for soil microbes, curbing pathogens and aiding nitrogen fixation [120]. Integrating agriculture with dairy, poultry, and other activities enriches soil fertility and cuts erosion risks [134]. These practices also create habitats for pollina tors and soil organisms, fostering disease resistance and plant growth [289]. Agrofor estry enhances biodiversity and soil quality, providing various ecosystem services such as moisture retention and nutrient availability [51, 77, 128, 129]. Additionally, organic manure application maintains soil fertility, with vermicompost providing additional benefits due to its nutrient-richness and support for microflora [69]. The sustainability and productivity of agroecosystems depend on the preservation of important micro bial processes. The distribution of microbial functional groups, which drive processes such as soil carbon storage and nutrient turnover, is shaped by management practices. In contrast, soil aggregates provide essential habitats that support their activity. Accord ing to Helgason et al. [100], the main ways to affect soil microbial populations are by changing the soil’s structure or adding substrates. Peixoto et al. [228] found that, based on 16 S rRNA gene analysis of bulk soils under conventional tillage (CT) and no-tillage (NT), the microbial community in NT soils more closely resembled that of nearby for ested soils than that of the conventionally tilled soil. They attributed this to changes in soil structure, such as an increase in the mean weight diameter of aggregates under NT. Recently, Muruganandam et al. [206] found that while the composition of the microbial community varied between aggregates from tilled and NT soils, it did not differ among aggregate-size classes. Farmers frequently see till age as a management technique for physically modifying soil to improve crop establish ment. Numerous ecosystem services and soil processes are affected when soil physical characteristics are altered [37, 160, 163]. In the long term, excessive tillage techniques may negatively affect soil health. These methods regulate soil organic matter oxidation, enhance porosity, and deter weed seed germination [119, 120]. Combining zero tillage with residue retention boosts rainwater infiltration by 8.3 to 21.5% [247], reduces runoff erosive potential by 19.3% [158], enhances aggregate stability by 5.8% [211], and organic carbon content by 15.9% [117] Adhering to conservation agriculture principles, includ ing soil cover and diversified cropping, reduces compaction, moderates soil tempera ture, and mitigates erosion [244]. Reducing synthetic pesticide use benefits beneficial organisms and pollinators; promoting organic or bio-pesticides improves soil biologi cal activity [18]. Conservation agriculture practices have been shown to improve soil moisture content significantly, mean weight diameter (MWD), and water-stable aggre gates (WSA > 0.25 mm), especially in the topsoil (0–15 cm). These practices also lead to notable reductions in bulk density and penetration resistance [118]. The extent to which low tillage and other practices affect the soil’s physical characteristics can vary by soil type and climate. Parihar et al. [225] observed that in semi-arid sandy loam soils, water stable aggregates increased by 16.1–32.5%, while bulk density and penetration resistance reduced significantly. Similar outcomes in sandy loam soil were also reported by Singh et al. [263] following five years of testing. In contrast, Somasundaram et al. [270] found that, after 4 years of testing, clay soil in central India’s hot sub-humid climate showed a 10% increase in WSA and a 20% increase in MWD compared with conventional tillage. The goal of RA is to produce more from less: “less land area, less input of chemicals, less use of water, less emission of greenhouse gases, less risk of soil degrada tion, and less use of energy-based inputs” [162]. It brings together economic, social, and environmental dimensions by emphasizing three core objectives: (1) achieving sustainable growth in agricultural productivity and incomes, (2) strengthening adaptation and resilience to climate change, and (3) reducing or eliminating greenhouse gas emissions [78]. Conservation agriculture is key to establish ing sustainable agro-ecosystems. Practices such as no-till systems, mulch crops, cover crops, nutrient management, and crop rotation contribute to soil and water conserva tion, carbon sequestration, and adaptation to local climate conditions [161]. A recent study found that a 1% increase in SOM enhanced available water holding capacity (AWHC) by up to 1.5%, depending on soil texture and mineralogy, with the effect more pronounced in sandy soils [169]. Through carbon sequestration and enhanced nitrogen usage efficiency, integrated nutrient management (INM) promotes soil fertility and agricultural productivity while mitigating the effects of climate change [219]. In contrast to conventional nutrient management in the rice-wheat cropping system, following straw centralized returning reduced CH4 emis sions in the rice season by 27.5% in China [168]. Practices such as mulching improve soil moisture, structure, and temperature, and reduce soil and nutrient loss [219]. In addition to increasing soil fertility, cover crops can sequester carbon. Their widespread use might lower agricultural GHG emissions by 10%, which is equivalent to the use of no-till or other cropping techniques [145]. Reducing agricultural greenhouse gas emis sions is achievable through improved practices in crop establishment, nutrient man agement, irrigation, tillage, and residue management [11, 195]. Together, these strategies aim to produce more grain with lower greenhouse gas emissions. Countries need to align their adaptation strategies with the Paris Agreement and enhance resilience by implementing strong, well-integrated agricultural policies [293]. Climate models project economic losses up to US$1.01 trillion by 2050, primarily affecting consumers through higher food prices [222]. Farmers often lack region-specific information on climate impacts and adaptation options, limiting their ability to cope with shifting growing seasons and pest pressures. Knowledge exchange and access to climate data, forecasts, and early warning systems are essential for effective response strategies [251]. However, only 25% of survey respon dents reported using resources from the Global Alliance for Climate-Smart Agriculture (GACSA), indicating limited dissemination even among actively involved stakeholders. Government policies play a critical role in promoting CSA by incentivizing sustain able practices, supporting resilient crop research, improving water and soil manage ment, and encouraging agroforestry [272]. Discover Soil (2026) 3:39 8.2 Crop rotations Long-term dependence on monoculture systems negatively affects soil microbial com munities by reducing the diversity of beneficial microorganisms and promoting the buildup of specific pathogens. Predominant fungal phyla include Ascomycota (62.5%), Glomeromycota (11.1%), Mucoromycota (8.9%), and Basidiomycota (6.8%), while dominant bacterial phyla are Pseudomonadota (35.0%), Actinomycetota (21.1%), and Bacillota (10.1%) [22, 112, 299]. Monoculture disrupts ecological balance, reduces pro ductivity and quality, and intensifies pest and disease outbreaks over time [124]. Climate change exacerbates these challenges by shifting species phenology and distributions, increasing pest pressure, and altering ecosystem dynamics [72]. In contrast, crop rotation enhances soil resilience and reduces erosion caused by extreme weather. For instance, in Jiangxi province, rotating alfalfa with corn increased yield by 1.5 t acre− 1 and improved soil aggregate stability by 35% [91]. Wang et al. [299] noted that rotation encourages macro-aggregate formation, improving erosion resis tance. It also enhances soil structure, water dynamics, biological functions, and crop resistance to climate stress. Notably, rotation improves agroecosystem biodiversity by increasing community composition (61%), bacterial species richness (69%), and soil multi-functionality (52%) ([25]; Li et al., 2021). Rotation also increases water-use effi ciency, reduces erosion, and improves water retention and drainage [6, 164, 298]. Its effectiveness is influenced by crop family, frequency (≥ 1 season), duration (up to 1 year), site history, and soil quality [31, 282]. Practices such as intercropping and crop rotation with suitable genotypes further enhance productivity and soil health [84, 311]. Accord ing to Xie et al., [305], intercropping Bletilla striata with peach trees in a silvo-medicinal system markedly increased soil nutrient availability, with total N, available N, available P, available K, and nitrate-N rising by 6.85%, 64.91%, 161.71%, 106.53%, and 16.82%, respectively, compared with monoculture. This system also elevated microbial activity, with notable increases in Acidobacteriota (0.17 ± 0.05 vs. 0.06 ± 0.03), Chloroflexi (0.12 ± 0.04 vs. 0.02 ± 0.00), and Basidiomycota (0.21 ± 0.12 vs. 0.09 ± 0.03). Similar find ings in orchard intercropping systems confirm improvements in microbial richness [60, 70, 315]. In summary, crop rotation is a vital agroecological practice that enhances soil health, increases biodiversity, improves climate resilience, and boosts overall productiv ity, making it integral to sustainable farming systems (Table 6). Crop diversification is crucial for boosting yields, maintaining soil health, and balanc ing soil biodiversity while reducing the burden of pathogens [25, 93]. According to a meta-analysis by Beillouin et al., [30], diversification increased biodiversity by 24% and crop yields by 14%. Additionally, the miscon ception that monoculture yields more biomass hinders diversification efforts. 9 Soil microbe-plant interactions and soil health The thriving health of the plant rhizosphere is attributed to a diverse array of benefi cial microorganisms, including rhizobacteria, mycorrhizal fungi, and others that sup port plant growth. Plant genotypes influence rhizosphere composition through root exudates, thereby attracting specific microbial species. Microorganisms coevolved with plants around 400 million years ago, transitioning from aquatic habitats to soil, estab lishing crucial soil-microbe-plant systems for nutrient cycling, maintaining soil fertility, and carbon sequestration [82]. Root exudates released by plants directly impact the root microbiome, providing car bon and energy to soil microorganisms and significantly altering soil physicochemi cal properties. These exudates also act as signaling molecules for soil microorganisms, including various plant hormones and defense chemicals, influencing plant-microbe interactions [123, 181, 189]. The mycorrhizal association between fungi and plant roots enhances nutrient absorption by increasing the root surface area [27, 141]. Agricultural land contamination with heavy metals from industrialization and urbanization has led to their biomagnification in ecosystems. Phytoremediation, combined with mycorrhizal fungi, offers a sustainable approach to remove heavy metals from soil, ensuring ecosys tem health [175, 187, 192]. Microbes also play critical roles in soil functions like organic matter decomposition, nutrient cycling, and plant growth regulation [98]. Understand ing the taxonomic structure of beneficial soil microorganisms is crucial for sustainable agriculture, and bioinformatics tools facilitate functional analysis by linking microbial community data with biological functions [82, 166, 214, 291]. 10 Indicators for evaluating soil health in cropping systems Farmers and stakeholders are increasingly embracing “soil health practices” to improve soil quality, recognizing their crucial role in achieving higher crop yields, protecting the environment, and mitigating climate change. These practices aim to ensure economic productivity and ecological sustainability, preserving or restoring the land’s integrity. Healthy soil underpins essential ecosystem functions, including supporting biological productivity, regulating air and water quality, and sustaining plant, animal, and human life. Simple and cost-effective methods are used to assess physical indicators of soil health, including bulk density, porosity, aggregate stability, and soil texture. Hydrological pro cesses such as runoff, aeration, erosion, infiltration rate, and water holding capacity are linked to these indicators [256]. Weak cohesion, low aeration and root density, high surface runoff, low water infiltration rates, and mechanization challenges are all signs of poor physical characteristics [67]. Indicators of chemical health, such as pH, CEC, organic matter content, and nutrient levels, are essential for determining how effectively soil retains toxic chemicals that impact the environment and plant development [146].Page 26 of 39 Joshi et al. The steady accumulation of organic matter from plants and animals enhances soil quality and helps sustain the natural cycles within ecosys tems. Effective utilization of organic carbon and minerals relies on their recycling within the ecosystem [17]. Microorganisms such as Rhizobium, Azotobacter, and Azospirillum increase nitrogen availability [217], while Pseudomonas, Bacillus, and Aspergillus solubilize phos phorus, thereby improving plant uptake [34]. Biofertilizers also produce phytohormones and enzymes that promote root development and suppress pathogens, reducing the need for pesticides [48, 217]. Their long-term application enhances soil structure, water retention, and microbial diversity [32], making them a vital component of sustainable agriculture [217]. The functional microbial-based bio-stimulants AMF and PGPR have been found to have favorable impacts on crops. AMFs and PGPR are involved in root colonization, boosting soil production and plant tolerance to abiotic challenges [132]. Similarly, Joel et al. Sustainable soil management is promoted through information exchange and capacity building, supported by initiatives such as the Global Soil Part nership (GSP) and the FAO’s World Soil Charter. Whereas the UNCCD aims to address land degradation and desertification, the 4 per 1000 Initiative concentrates on raising soil organic carbon to combat global warming. Policies such as the EU Soil Directive and the Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN) framework guide nations in soil conserva tion, encouraging practices such as agroforestry, RA, and soil restoration. These efforts highlight the collective global responsibility for preserving soil health. Organizations like FAO and UNEP play pivotal roles in global soil health advocacy. FAO leads through ini tiatives such as the Global Soil Information System (GLOSIS) and Soil Health Card pro grams, while UNEP integrates soil conservation into biodiversity and climate policies. Case studies demonstrate regional successes, such as India’s Soil Health Card Scheme, China’s Grain for Green Program, and Brazil’s no-till farming practices. These examples emphasize the need for policy-driven approaches supported by international collabo ration. Together, they provide a blueprint for sustainable soil management that bal ances food security, environmental conservation, and climate resilience across diverse ecosystems. 13 Challenges and barriers to implementing sustainable soil management practices 13.1 Economic, social, and institutional constraints In many degraded and rainfed areas, farmers often get trapped in cycles where poverty and land degradation reinforce each other [24, 89, 193, 255]. Low productivity leads to low income, which in turn limits farmers’ capacity to invest in soil conservation [152,Page 28 of 39 Joshi et al. Discover Soil (2026) 3:39 201]. Their limited financial resources compel them to cultivate low-value crops, rely on traditional inputs, and refrain from adopting improved practices such as using high quality seeds or installing irrigation systems. Poor access to credit, weak institutions, and poverty-induced migration further erode human and social capital, hindering awareness and uptake of technology. These systemic issues necessitate technological support and targeted investments to break the cycle of degradation and poverty. 13.2 Technological and infrastructure barriers Soil degradation varies across locations, requiring tailored management interventions rather than a universal solution [114]. Many sustainable soil management approaches, such as watershed development and subsurface drainage, require community partici pation because of the interconnected hydrology. Adoption is also constrained by weak enabling infrastructure, including limited market access and a lack of extension services. Improving access to inputs and ensuring fair pricing through better market connections is key to increasing adoption and boosting productivity. 13.3 Policy, tenure, and governance issues Tenure insecurity remains a major deterrent to conservation investment, especially by tenant farmers who see limited long-term returns [152, 200]. Heavy reliance on subsidies (e.g., 80–90% in watershed and drainage projects) discourages private investment, as farmers await public schemes. This calls for shifting toward credit-based soil restoration business models, with government agencies focusing on technical support, particularly in severely affected regions (e.g., subsurface drainage in sugarcane areas of Maharash tra). Despite the benefits of soil conservation for ecosystem services, payment for eco system services (PES) remains insufficiently embedded in planning processes, thereby restricting the economic incentives necessary for sustainable land management [155]. 13.4 Knowledge gaps and research needs Natural resource management (NRM) technologies often offer delayed but long-term benefits that farmers may not fully recognize. For instance, watershed interventions often take 5–7 years to fully deliver their benefits, and the ecosystem services they pro vide extend beyond individual farms [155]. Research should prioritize breaking the deg radation–poverty nexus, now worsened by climate change impacts, through resilient, climate-smart technologies [153]. There is a need for inclusive, cost-effective, and scal able soil management strategies that engage smallholders through action research and participatory development. Policies must be tailored to socioeconomic realities, with a focus on integrating private investment and community participation alongside public support. 14 Conclusion and future thrust This review demonstrates that soil health management is fundamental to sustain ing agricultural productivity under accelerating climate change. Diverse soil biological communities, particularly AMF, PGPR, cyanobacteria, and other beneficial microbes, regulate nutrient cycling, carbon sequestration, soil aggregation, and root–soil interac tions that underpin ecosystem functioning. Climate change–induced abiotic stresses such as drought, salinity, heat waves, nutrient imbalances, and soil degradation intensifyPage 29 of 39 Joshi et al. Discover Soil (2026) 3:39 vulnerabilities in cropping systems, making biologically driven soil processes even more critical. Evidence from conservation agriculture, regenerative agriculture, crop diver sification, reduced tillage, intercropping, and INM confirms that these practices sig nificantly enhance microbial diversity, soil organic matter, water retention, and plant resilience. Policy interventions, institutional capacity, and advances in technologies such as precision agriculture, remote sensing, and biofertilizers further strengthen the poten tial for soil-centered climate resilience. Collectively, the synthesis highlights that restor ing and maintaining soil health is foundational for achieving sustainable productivity, ecological stability, and food security in the face of escalating climatic risks. Future research must focus on integrating soil biological processes more deeply into climate-resilient agricultural planning. Priority areas include (i) long-term, field-scale evaluation of AMF-PGPR consortia under multi-stress environments such as com bined drought-heat, salinity-drought, and waterlogging scenarios; (ii) development of robust microbial formulations with greater shelf life, stress tolerance, and compatibil ity with major cropping systems; and (iii) advanced monitoring tools using soil sensors, UAVs, and GIS for real-time assessment of soil biological and physicochemical indica tors. Strengthening policy frameworks to reward ecosystem services, especially carbon sequestration, soil restoration, and watershed conservation, will be essential for large scale adoption. Greater emphasis is needed on climate-smart extension models, com munity-based soil restoration programs, and blended financial mechanisms that attract private sector participation. Finally, future soil health strategies must embrace a systems perspective, integrating microbial technologies, regenerative practices, and socio-insti tutional innovations to build resilient, productive, and sustainable agroecosystems capa ble of withstanding the uncertainties of climate change.
